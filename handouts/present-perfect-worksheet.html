üìö Introduction: Present Perfect

The Big Idea

Present Perfect connects your past experiences to right now: projects you've done, habits that started before today, and fresh results you can still see.

Real-Life Uses:

  • Experiences (no specific time): "I've led three product launches."
  • Actions that continue to now: "She has worked remotely since 2021."
  • Recent actions with present results: "We've just deployed the fix‚Äîit's live."

Formula: have/has + past participle

‚≠ê When Do We Use Present Perfect?

The key question: "Is this connected to NOW?"

1. Life Experiences: Things you've done at some point (no exact time). Ex: "I have been to Paris three times."

2. Actions That Continue NOW: Started in past, still true (use 'for' or 'since'). Ex: "I have worked here since 2021."

3. Recent Actions: Just happened, see result now (use 'just', 'already', 'yet'). Ex: "She has already sent it."

4. Multiple Times: Repeated actions up to now. Ex: "I have visited four times today."

5. Past Action ‚Üí Present Result: Something happened ‚Üí see result NOW. Ex: "I have lost my keys."

‚öñÔ∏è Present Perfect vs. Past Simple

This is one of the most confusing parts for English learners! Both tenses talk about the past, but they have different meanings.

Side-by-Side Comparison

Present Perfect Past Simple
Time Focus Connects past to PRESENT - time is NOT specific Finished action in the PAST - time IS specific
Time Words ever, never, already, yet, just, still, for, since yesterday, last week, in 2020, ago, when I was young
Example I have visited Paris. (Sometime in my life - when is not important) I visited Paris in 2019. (Specific time - finished action)
Example She has lived here for 5 years. (Started in past, STILL lives here now) She lived here for 5 years. (Doesn't live here anymore - finished)
Example Have you seen this movie? (Ever in your life?) Did you see this movie last night? (Specific time)

Step 1: How to Form Present Perfect (Positive)

Formula: Subject + have/has + past participle | Examples: I have finished. She has been to Paris. They have lived here for 5 years.

‚è≥ For vs Since

Use 'for' to show a duration and 'since' to show the starting point.

FOR = Duration (for 5 years, for a week). Ex: "I have studied English for 3 years."

SINCE = Starting point (since 2020, since Monday). Ex: "I have lived here since 2020."

‚è∞ Already, Just, Yet, or Still?

ALREADY = Earlier than expected (positive). Ex: "I have already finished."

JUST = Very recently. Ex: "I have just arrived home."

YET = Expected but hasn't happened (questions/negatives). Ex: "Have you finished yet?" "I haven't eaten yet."

STILL = Continues to not happen (before haven't/hasn't). Ex: "I still haven't finished."

üåü Ever vs Never

EVER = At any time in your life (usually in questions). Ex: "Have you ever been to Paris?"

NEVER = Not at any time in your life. Ex: "I have never seen snow."

‚úì Summary: Key Points to Remember

What You've Learned

  • Form: Present Perfect = have/has + past participle
  • Meaning: Connects the PAST to the PRESENT (not finished, or important now)
  • 5 Main Uses:
    1. Life experiences (no specific time)
    2. Unfinished actions (started in past, continue to now)
    3. Recent actions (just, already, yet)
    4. Multiple times in life
    5. Past actions with present results
  • Time Expressions: for, since, already, yet, just, still, ever, never
  • Negative: haven't/hasn't + past participle
  • Questions: Have/Has + subject + past participle?
  • vs Past Simple: Present Perfect = connection to NOW. Past Simple = finished action with specific time.

üí° Final Tip: Always ask yourself: "Is this connected to the present?" If yes, use Present Perfect!
