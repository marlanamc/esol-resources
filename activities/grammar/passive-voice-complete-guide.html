What is Passive Voice? Passive voice is a way to talk about actions when the focus is on what happened rather than who did it. It's very common in formal writing, scientific texts, and news reports.

Active Voice vs Passive Voice - Understanding the Difference

Why is this important?

Understanding the difference between active and passive voice helps you know when and why to use passive voice. In active voice, the subject does the action. In passive voice, the subject receives the action.

üü¢ Active Voice

Subject does the action

The chef cooked the meal.
Maria wrote the letter.
They built the house.

üí° Focus: WHO did the action

üîµ Passive Voice

Subject receives the action

The meal was cooked (by the chef).
The letter was written (by Maria).
The house was built (by them).

üí° Focus: WHAT happened

üí° Key Point:

In passive voice, we often don't mention who did the action because:

  • We don't know who did it ("The window was broken")
  • It's not important who did it ("English is spoken worldwide")
  • It's obvious who did it ("The thief was arrested")

When Do We Use Passive Voice?

When should you use passive voice?

Passive voice is used in specific situations. Knowing when to use it makes your English more natural and appropriate!

üìå We use passive voice when:

  • 1. We don't know who did the action
    My car was stolen last night. (We don't know who stole it)
    The window was broken. (We don't know who broke it)
  • 2. It's not important who did the action
    English is spoken all over the world. (Who speaks it doesn't matter)
    The report was submitted yesterday. (Who submitted it isn't the focus)
  • 3. We want to focus on the action or result
    The new hospital was opened last month. (Focus on the hospital, not who opened it)
    The problem was solved quickly. (Focus on the solution)
  • 4. In formal or scientific writing
    The experiment was conducted in a controlled environment.
    The data was analyzed using statistical methods.
  • 5. In news reports
    The president was elected yesterday.
    Three people were injured in the accident.
    • üí° Key Point:

    Use passive voice when the action or result is more important than who did it. In everyday conversation, active voice is usually more natural, but passive voice is very common in formal writing, science, and news.

    Present Simple Passive

    How to form Present Simple Passive

    Present Simple Passive is used to talk about things that are generally done or always true in passive form.

    Subject + am/is/are + past participle + (by + agent)
    ‚ö†Ô∏è Use am for I, is for he/she/it, are for you/we/they
    Look at these examples:
    English is spoken all over the world.
    Coffee is grown in Brazil.
    The letters are delivered every morning.
    I am paid every Friday.
    The car is washed every week.
    üí° Remember:
    • I ‚Üí am + past participle
    • He/She/It ‚Üí is + past participle
    • You/We/They ‚Üí are + past participle

    Past Simple Passive

    How to form Past Simple Passive

    Past Simple Passive is used to talk about actions that were done in the past.

    Subject + was/were + past participle + (by + agent)
    ‚ö†Ô∏è Use was for I/he/she/it, were for you/we/they
    Look at these examples:
    The house was built in 1990.
    The letter was written yesterday.
    The windows were broken during the storm.
    I was invited to the party.
    The books were sold last week.
    üí° Remember:
    • I/He/She/It ‚Üí was + past participle
    • You/We/They ‚Üí were + past participle

    Present Perfect Passive

    How to form Present Perfect Passive

    Present Perfect Passive is used to talk about actions that have been completed recently or have a connection to the present.

    Subject + have/has been + past participle + (by + agent)
    ‚ö†Ô∏è Use have been for I/you/we/they, has been for he/she/it
    Look at these examples:
    The report has been finished.
    The windows have been cleaned.
    I have been invited to three parties this month.
    The car has been repaired.
    The documents have been signed.
    üí° Remember:
    • I/You/We/They ‚Üí have been + past participle
    • He/She/It ‚Üí has been + past participle

    Future Passive

    How to form Future Passive

    Future Passive is used to talk about actions that will be done in the future.

    Subject + will be + past participle + (by + agent)
    ‚ö†Ô∏è Will be is used for all subjects (I, you, he, she, it, we, they)
    Look at these examples:
    The house will be built next year.
    The report will be finished by Friday.
    You will be informed about the results.
    The car will be repaired tomorrow.
    The meeting will be held next week.
    üí° Remember:

    Future Passive uses will be + past participle for all subjects. It's the same for I, you, he, she, it, we, and they!

    Modal Passives

    How to form Modal Passives

    Modal Passives combine modals (can, should, must, etc.) with passive voice to express possibility, obligation, or ability in passive form.

    Subject + modal + be + past participle
    ‚ö†Ô∏è Common modals: can, should, must, might, may, could, have to, need to
    Look at these examples:
    The problem can be solved easily.
    This work should be finished by tomorrow.
    The door must be locked at night.
    The report might be delayed.
    The car needs to be repaired.
    üí° Remember:
    • Modal + be + past participle
    • Can be, should be, must be, might be, may be, could be
    • Have to be, need to be (with "to")

    Passive with Reporting Verbs

    How to use Passive with Reporting Verbs

    Some reporting verbs (say, believe, think, know, etc.) are commonly used in passive form, especially in formal writing and news.

    It + is/was said/believed/thought + that + clause
    Subject + is/was said/believed/thought + to + infinitive
    Look at these examples:
    It is said that he is very rich.
    He is said to be very rich.
    It is believed that the building is 200 years old.
    The building is believed to be 200 years old.
    It was thought that she had left.
    She was thought to have left.
    üí° Common Reporting Verbs in Passive:
    • say ‚Üí is/was said
    • believe ‚Üí is/was believed
    • think ‚Üí is/was thought
    • know ‚Üí is/was known
    • expect ‚Üí is/was expected
    • report ‚Üí is/was reported

    Converting Active to Passive

    How to convert Active to Passive

    Follow these steps to convert an active sentence to passive:

  • Step 1: Make the object of the active sentence the subject of the passive sentence
  • Step 2: Use the appropriate form of "be" (am/is/are/was/were/have been/has been/will be)
  • Step 3: Use the past participle of the main verb
  • Step 4: Add "by + agent" if you want to mention who did the action (optional)
    • Examples:
    Active: The chef cooked the meal.
    Passive: The meal was cooked (by the chef).
    Active: They are building a new bridge.
    Passive: A new bridge is being built (by them).
    Active: She has finished the report.
    Passive: The report has been finished (by her).
    üí° Remember:

    Not all sentences can be made passive! The sentence must have a direct object. Intransitive verbs (verbs without objects) cannot be passive.

    Example: "I sleep" cannot be passive because "sleep" has no object.
