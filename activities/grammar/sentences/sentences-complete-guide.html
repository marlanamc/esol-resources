What is a sentence? A sentence is a group of words that expresses a complete thought. It has a subject and a verb, and it makes sense on its own.

Question Forms

What are questions?

Questions are sentences that ask for information. There are many different types of questions in English, and each has its own structure.

üìå Types of Questions:

  • 1. Yes/No Questions - Questions that can be answered with "yes" or "no"
    Do you like coffee? ‚Üí Yes, I do. / No, I don't.
    Is she a teacher? ‚Üí Yes, she is. / No, she isn't.
    Can you swim? ‚Üí Yes, I can. / No, I can't.

    üí° Structure: Auxiliary verb (do/does/did/is/are/can) + subject + main verb + ?

  • 2. Wh- Questions - Questions that start with question words (what, where, when, who, why, how)
    What is your name? ‚Üí My name is Maria.
    Where do you live? ‚Üí I live in Boston.
    When did you arrive? ‚Üí I arrived yesterday.
    Why are you studying English? ‚Üí Because I need it for work.

    üí° Structure: Question word + auxiliary verb + subject + main verb + ?

  • 3. Question Tags - Short questions added to the end of statements
    You like coffee, don't you? ‚Üí Yes, I do.
    She isn't coming, is she? ‚Üí No, she isn't.
    They can swim, can't they? ‚Üí Yes, they can.

    üí° Rule: If the statement is positive, the tag is negative. If the statement is negative, the tag is positive.

  • 4. Indirect Questions - Polite questions that start with phrases like "Could you tell me...", "Do you know..."
    Direct: Where is the station?
    Indirect: Could you tell me where the station is?
    Direct: What time does it start?
    Indirect: Do you know what time it starts?

    üí° Note: In indirect questions, the word order changes - no question mark inversion!

  • 5. Subject vs Object Questions - Questions about the subject vs questions about the object
    Subject question: Who called you? (Who = subject, no auxiliary verb)
    Object question: Who did you call? (Who = object, needs auxiliary verb)

    üí° Subject questions don't use auxiliary verbs. Object questions do!

  • 6. Embedded Questions - Questions inside other sentences
    I wonder where she went.
    Can you tell me what time it is?
    I don't know why he left.

    üí° Embedded questions use statement word order (subject + verb), not question word order.

  • 7. Negative Questions - Questions with "not"
    Don't you like coffee? (surprised or expecting yes)
    Isn't she coming? (surprised or expecting yes)
    Haven't you finished yet? (surprised or expecting yes)

    üí° Negative questions often show surprise or expectation.

    Statements

    What are statements?

    Statements are sentences that give information. They can be affirmative (positive) or negative.

    üìå Types of Statements:

  • 1. Affirmative Statements - Positive statements that say something is true
    I like coffee.
    She works at a hospital.
    They study English every day.

    üí° Structure: Subject + verb + (object/complement)

  • 2. Negative Statements - Statements that say something is not true
    I don't like coffee.
    She doesn't work here.
    They didn't come yesterday.

    üí° Structure: Subject + don't/doesn't/didn't + base verb + (object)

  • 3. Double Negatives - Using two negative words (usually incorrect in English)
    ‚ùå I don't have no money. (incorrect)
    ‚úÖ I don't have any money. (correct)
    ‚ùå I can't see nothing. (incorrect)
    ‚úÖ I can't see anything. (correct)

    üí° In English, we avoid double negatives. Use "any" instead of "no" after "don't/doesn't".

  • 4. Statement Word Order - The correct order of words in statements
    Subject + Verb + Object/Complement
    I (subject) + eat (verb) + breakfast (object).
    She (subject) + is (verb) + a teacher (complement).

    Imperatives

    What are imperatives?

    Imperatives are sentences that give commands, instructions, or make requests. They usually don't have a subject - the subject "you" is understood.

    üìå Types of Imperatives:

  • 1. Positive Imperatives - Commands or requests to do something
    Close the door.
    Please sit down.
    Be quiet!

    üí° Structure: (Please) + base verb + (object)

  • 2. Negative Imperatives - Commands or requests NOT to do something
    Don't close the door.
    Please don't smoke here.
    Don't be late!

    üí° Structure: Don't + base verb + (object)

  • 3. Polite Imperatives - Using "please" or modal verbs to be more polite
    Please close the window.
    Could you help me?
    Would you mind opening the door?

    üí° Adding "please" or using modals makes imperatives more polite.

  • 4. Sequence Words in Imperatives - Using words like "first", "then", "next", "finally"
    First, mix the ingredients. Then, bake for 30 minutes. Finally, let it cool.
    Next, add the sugar. After that, stir well.

    üí° Sequence words help organize instructions in order.

    Simple Sentences

    What are simple sentences?

    Simple sentences have one independent clause. They contain a subject and a verb and express a complete thought.

    Subject + Verb + (Object/Complement)
    Examples:
    I (subject) + eat (verb) + breakfast (object).
    She (subject) + is (verb) + a teacher (complement).
    They (subject) + study (verb) + English (object).
    2

    Compound Sentences

    What are compound sentences?

    Compound sentences join two or more independent clauses with coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, so).

    Independent Clause 1 + Coordinating Conjunction + Independent Clause 2
    Coordinating Conjunctions:
    And - addition
    But - contrast
    Or - choice
    So - result
    Examples:
    I like coffee, and she likes tea.
    He wanted to go, but he was too tired.
    You can stay here, or you can leave.
    It was raining, so we stayed inside.
    üí° Punctuation:

    Use a comma before the coordinating conjunction when joining two independent clauses.

    3

    Complex Sentences

    What are complex sentences?

    Complex sentences have one independent clause and one or more dependent (subordinate) clauses. Dependent clauses cannot stand alone.

    Independent Clause + Subordinating Conjunction + Dependent Clause

    üìå Types of Dependent Clauses:

  • 1. Adverbial Clauses - Clauses that function as adverbs (time, reason, purpose, result, contrast)
    Time: I'll call you when I arrive.
    Reason: She left early because she was tired.
    Purpose: I studied hard so that I could pass.
    Result: It was so cold that I wore a coat.
    Contrast: Although it was raining, we went outside.
  • 2. Noun Clauses - Clauses that function as nouns
    I know that you are right.
    I wonder where she went.
    Tell me what you want.
  • 3. Adjective Clauses (Relative Clauses) - Clauses that describe nouns
    The book that I bought is interesting.
    The woman who lives next door is a doctor.
    The place where we met is closed now.
    • 4

    Compound-Complex Sentences

    What are compound-complex sentences?

    Compound-complex sentences combine compound and complex structures. They have at least two independent clauses and at least one dependent clause.

    Examples:
    I wanted to go to the party, but I stayed home because I was sick.
    She studied hard, and she passed the exam that was very difficult.
    Although it was late, we continued working, and we finished the project.

    Linking Words and Connectors

    What are linking words?

    Linking words (connectors) connect ideas and show relationships between parts of sentences or between sentences.

    üìå Types of Connectors:

  • 1. Addition Connectors - Add more information
    And, Moreover, Furthermore, In addition, Also
    I like coffee. Moreover, I like tea.
    She speaks English. In addition, she speaks Spanish.
  • 2. Contrast Connectors - Show difference or opposition
    But, However, Although, Despite, In spite of, Whereas
    I wanted to go, but I was too tired.
    It was raining. However, we went outside.
    Although it was cold, we went swimming.
  • 3. Cause and Effect Connectors - Show reason and result
    Because, So, Therefore, As a result, Consequently, Since
    It was raining, so we stayed inside.
    She studied hard. Therefore, she passed the exam.
    Because I was tired, I went to bed early.
  • 4. Sequence Connectors - Show order or time
    First, Second, Then, Next, Finally, After that, Meanwhile
    First, mix the ingredients. Then, bake for 30 minutes. Finally, let it cool.

    Sentence Types by Function

    What are sentence functions?

    Sentences can be classified by their function - what they do or what purpose they serve.

  • 1. Declarative Sentences - Make statements or give information (end with a period)
    I like coffee.
    She is a teacher.
  • 2. Interrogative Sentences - Ask questions (end with a question mark)
    Do you like coffee?
    Where is she?
  • 3. Imperative Sentences - Give commands or make requests (end with a period or exclamation mark)
    Close the door.
    Please sit down.
  • 4. Exclamatory Sentences - Express strong emotion (end with an exclamation mark)
    What a beautiful day!
    How amazing!

    Sentence Structure Issues

    Common Sentence Problems

    Here are common mistakes to avoid when writing sentences.

  • 1. Run-on Sentences - Two or more independent clauses joined incorrectly
    ‚ùå I like coffee she likes tea. (incorrect - missing punctuation)
    ‚úÖ I like coffee, and she likes tea. (correct)
    ‚úÖ I like coffee. She likes tea. (correct)
  • 2. Sentence Fragments - Incomplete sentences that don't express a complete thought
    ‚ùå Because I was tired. (fragment - incomplete thought)
    ‚úÖ Because I was tired, I went to bed early. (complete sentence)
  • 3. Parallel Structure - Using the same grammatical form for items in a list
    ‚ùå I like reading, to write, and swimming. (not parallel)
    ‚úÖ I like reading, writing, and swimming. (parallel - all gerunds)
    ‚úÖ I like to read, to write, and to swim. (parallel - all infinitives)
  • 4. Sentence Variety - Using different sentence types and lengths
    Good writing uses a mix of simple, compound, and complex sentences.
    Vary sentence length to keep writing interesting.
