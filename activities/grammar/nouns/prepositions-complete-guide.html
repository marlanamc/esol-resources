Welcome! Let's Learn About Prepositions Together

We're going to learn about prepositions step by step. Don't worry - we'll practice each part before moving to the next. Take your time and work through each section!

What is a preposition? A preposition is a word that shows the relationship between a noun or pronoun and other words in a sentence. Prepositions tell us about place (where), time (when), movement (direction), and other relationships. Examples: in, on, at, with, for, from.

1

Prepositions of Place

What are prepositions of place?

Prepositions of place tell us where something is located. They answer the question "Where?" The most common prepositions of place are: in, on, at, under, over, above, below, beside, between, among, behind, in front of, next to, near, far from.

üìù IN, ON, AT - The Most Common Prepositions

IN - Used for:
‚Ä¢ Enclosed spaces: in the box, in the room, in the car
‚Ä¢ Countries, cities, regions: in France, in London, in the north
‚Ä¢ General locations: in the park, in the building
ON - Used for:
‚Ä¢ Surfaces: on the table, on the wall, on the floor
‚Ä¢ Public transport: on the bus, on the train, on the plane
‚Ä¢ Days: on Monday, on weekends
AT - Used for:
‚Ä¢ Specific points: at the door, at the corner, at the station
‚Ä¢ Events: at the party, at the meeting
‚Ä¢ Times: at 3 o'clock, at night

üìù Other Common Prepositions of Place

under - below something (The cat is under the table.)
over - above, covering (The bridge goes over the river.)
above - higher than (The picture is above the sofa.)
below - lower than (The temperature is below zero.)
beside / next to - at the side of (Sit beside me. / The bank is next to the post office.)
between - in the middle of two things (The book is between the two boxes.)
among - surrounded by many things (She was among the crowd.)
behind - at the back of (The car is behind the house.)
in front of - before, ahead of (The tree is in front of the building.)
near - close to (The school is near my house.)
far from - not close to (The airport is far from the city center.)
üí° Key Point:

Remember: IN = inside/enclosed, ON = surface, AT = specific point. Think: "in a box, on a table, at a door."

Prepositions of Time

What are prepositions of time?

Prepositions of time tell us when something happens. They answer the question "When?" The most common prepositions of time are: in, on, at, for, since, during, before, after, until, by, from...to.

üìù IN, ON, AT - Time Prepositions

IN - Used for:
‚Ä¢ Months: in January, in March
‚Ä¢ Years: in 2024, in 1990
‚Ä¢ Seasons: in spring, in summer
‚Ä¢ Parts of day: in the morning, in the afternoon, in the evening
‚Ä¢ Periods: in a week, in two hours
ON - Used for:
‚Ä¢ Days: on Monday, on Tuesday
‚Ä¢ Dates: on January 1st, on my birthday
‚Ä¢ Special days: on Christmas Day, on weekends
AT - Used for:
‚Ä¢ Clock times: at 3 o'clock, at 9:30
‚Ä¢ Specific times: at night, at midnight, at noon
‚Ä¢ Mealtimes: at breakfast, at lunch, at dinner
‚Ä¢ Holidays: at Christmas, at Easter

üìù Other Common Prepositions of Time

for - duration (I've lived here for 5 years.)
since - starting point (I've worked here since 2020.)
during - throughout a period (It rained during the night.)
before - earlier than (Finish your homework before dinner.)
after - later than (We'll eat after the meeting.)
until / till - up to a time (Wait until 5 o'clock.)
by - no later than (Finish by Friday.)
from...to - start to end (Work from 9am to 5pm.)
üí° Key Point:

Remember: IN = months/years/seasons, ON = days/dates, AT = specific times. Use "for" with periods, "since" with starting points.

3

Prepositions of Movement

What are prepositions of movement?

Prepositions of movement show direction or movement from one place to another. They answer the question "Where to?" or "Where from?" Common prepositions of movement include: to, from, into, out of, onto, off, up, down, across, through, along, around, over, under, towards, away from.

üìù Common Prepositions of Movement

to - direction towards (I'm going to the store.)
from - starting point (She came from Paris.)
into - movement inside (He walked into the room.)
out of - movement outside (She ran out of the building.)
onto - movement onto a surface (The cat jumped onto the table.)
off - movement away from a surface (Get off the bus.)
up - movement higher (Walk up the stairs.)
down - movement lower (Go down the hill.)
across - from one side to the other (Walk across the street.)
through - movement inside something (Drive through the tunnel.)
along - movement beside (Walk along the river.)
around - movement in a circle (Walk around the park.)
over - movement above (The plane flew over the city.)
under - movement below (The car went under the bridge.)
towards - direction (Walk towards the station.)
away from - movement from (Run away from the danger.)
üí° Key Point:

Prepositions of movement show direction. "To" = destination, "from" = origin, "into" = entering, "out of" = exiting.

Prepositional Phrases

What are prepositional phrases?

A prepositional phrase is a group of words that begins with a preposition and ends with a noun or pronoun (called the object). Prepositional phrases act as adjectives or adverbs in sentences.

üìù Structure of Prepositional Phrases

Preposition + Object (noun/pronoun)
Examples:
‚Ä¢ in the box (preposition: in, object: box)
‚Ä¢ on the table (preposition: on, object: table)
‚Ä¢ at 3 o'clock (preposition: at, object: 3 o'clock)
‚Ä¢ with my friends (preposition: with, object: friends)
‚Ä¢ for five years (preposition: for, object: years)

üìù How Prepositional Phrases Work

As adjectives (modify nouns):
The book on the table is mine. (Which book? The one on the table.)
The girl with red hair is my sister. (Which girl? The one with red hair.)
As adverbs (modify verbs):
She arrived at 3 o'clock. (When did she arrive? At 3 o'clock.)
He lives in London. (Where does he live? In London.)
üí° Key Point:

Prepositional phrases = preposition + object. They can describe nouns (which one?) or verbs (when/where/how?).

5

Dependent Prepositions

What are dependent prepositions?

Dependent prepositions are prepositions that always follow specific verbs, adjectives, or nouns. These combinations are fixed - you must learn which preposition goes with which word!

üìù Common Verb + Preposition Combinations

depend on - I depend on my family.
believe in - Do you believe in ghosts?
listen to - Listen to the music.
wait for - Wait for me!
look at - Look at that bird.
look for - I'm looking for my keys.
think about - Think about the problem.
talk to / talk with - Talk to your teacher.
agree with - I agree with you.
apologize for - Apologize for being late.

üìù Common Adjective + Preposition Combinations

interested in - I'm interested in art.
good at - She's good at math.
afraid of - He's afraid of spiders.
proud of - I'm proud of you.
famous for - Paris is famous for the Eiffel Tower.
different from - This is different from that.
responsible for - Who's responsible for this?
married to - She's married to John.
üí° Key Point:

Dependent prepositions are fixed combinations. You must memorize them! "Depend on", "interested in", "good at" - these are always used together.

Prepositions + Gerunds

What are gerunds?

A gerund is a verb form ending in -ing that acts as a noun. When a preposition is followed by a verb, the verb must be in gerund form (verb + -ing).

üìù Preposition + Gerund Rule

Preposition + Verb + -ing
Examples:
‚Ä¢ I'm interested in learning English. (NOT: in learn)
‚Ä¢ She's good at playing tennis. (NOT: at play)
‚Ä¢ He's afraid of flying. (NOT: of fly)
‚Ä¢ Thank you for helping me. (NOT: for help)
‚Ä¢ I'm tired of waiting. (NOT: of wait)
‚Ä¢ She's thinking about moving to Paris. (NOT: about move)

üìù Common Patterns

After prepositions in dependent combinations:
I'm looking forward to seeing you. (to + seeing)
She apologized for being late. (for + being)
After prepositions in phrases:
Before leaving, turn off the lights. (before + leaving)
After finishing work, I go home. (after + finishing)
üí° Key Point:

After a preposition, always use a gerund (verb + -ing), never the base form of the verb. Preposition + verb-ing is the rule!

Common Preposition Errors

Common mistakes with prepositions

Many English learners make mistakes with prepositions. Here are the most common errors and how to avoid them!

üìù Common Errors to Avoid

Error 1: Using wrong preposition
‚ùå I'm waiting on the bus.
‚úÖ I'm waiting for the bus.
Error 2: Missing preposition
‚ùå I arrived the station.
‚úÖ I arrived at the station.
Error 3: Using infinitive instead of gerund after preposition
‚ùå I'm interested to learn English.
‚úÖ I'm interested in learning English.
Error 4: Confusing "in", "on", "at"
‚ùå I'll see you in Monday.
‚úÖ I'll see you on Monday.
‚ùå The meeting is on 3 o'clock.
‚úÖ The meeting is at 3 o'clock.
Error 5: Confusing "for" and "since"
‚ùå I've lived here since five years.
‚úÖ I've lived here for five years.
‚ùå I've worked here for 2020.
‚úÖ I've worked here since 2020.
üí° Key Point:

Remember: "for" + period of time, "since" + starting point. After prepositions, use gerunds (verb + -ing). Always check which preposition goes with which verb/adjective!
