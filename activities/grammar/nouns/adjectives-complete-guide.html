Welcome! Let's Learn About Adjectives Together

We're going to learn about adjectives step by step. Don't worry - we'll practice each part before moving to the next. Take your time and work through each section!

What is an adjective? An adjective is a word that describes or modifies a noun. It tells us more about the noun - what it's like, how it looks, feels, or behaves. Examples: big, beautiful, happy, tall, interesting, red.

1

Comparative Adjectives

What are comparative adjectives?

Comparative adjectives compare two things or people. They show which one has more or less of a quality. We use "than" after comparative adjectives.

üìù How to Form Comparatives

Short adjectives (1-2 syllables) adjective + -er
Long adjectives (3+ syllables) more + adjective
Short Adjectives (add -er):
tall ‚Üí taller (The building is taller than the tree.)
big ‚Üí bigger (This box is bigger than that one.)
happy ‚Üí happier (She is happier than her sister.)
easy ‚Üí easier (Math is easier than physics.)
Long Adjectives (use "more"):
beautiful ‚Üí more beautiful (The sunset is more beautiful than the sunrise.)
interesting ‚Üí more interesting (This book is more interesting than that one.)
comfortable ‚Üí more comfortable (This chair is more comfortable than that one.)
expensive ‚Üí more expensive (The car is more expensive than the bike.)

üìù Spelling Rules for -er

Rule 1: Add -er
tall ‚Üí taller, fast ‚Üí faster, small ‚Üí smaller
Rule 2: Ends in -e ‚Üí add -r
nice ‚Üí nicer, large ‚Üí larger, safe ‚Üí safer
Rule 3: Ends in -y ‚Üí change -y to -ier
happy ‚Üí happier, easy ‚Üí easier, busy ‚Üí busier
Rule 4: One vowel + one consonant ‚Üí double consonant + -er
big ‚Üí bigger, hot ‚Üí hotter, thin ‚Üí thinner
üí° Key Point:

Use comparative adjectives with "than" to compare two things. Short adjectives = add -er. Long adjectives = use "more".

2

Superlative Adjectives

What are superlative adjectives?

Superlative adjectives compare three or more things or people. They show which one has the most or least of a quality. We use "the" before superlative adjectives.

üìù How to Form Superlatives

Short adjectives (1-2 syllables) the + adjective + -est
Long adjectives (3+ syllables) the + most + adjective
Short Adjectives (add -est):
tall ‚Üí the tallest (He is the tallest person in the room.)
big ‚Üí the biggest (This is the biggest building in the city.)
happy ‚Üí the happiest (She is the happiest person I know.)
easy ‚Üí the easiest (This is the easiest test I've taken.)
Long Adjectives (use "the most"):
beautiful ‚Üí the most beautiful (She is the most beautiful woman in the world.)
interesting ‚Üí the most interesting (This is the most interesting book I've read.)
comfortable ‚Üí the most comfortable (This is the most comfortable chair.)
expensive ‚Üí the most expensive (That is the most expensive car in the showroom.)

üìù Spelling Rules for -est

Rule 1: Add -est
tall ‚Üí the tallest, fast ‚Üí the fastest, small ‚Üí the smallest
Rule 2: Ends in -e ‚Üí add -st
nice ‚Üí the nicest, large ‚Üí the largest, safe ‚Üí the safest
Rule 3: Ends in -y ‚Üí change -y to -iest
happy ‚Üí the happiest, easy ‚Üí the easiest, busy ‚Üí the busiest
Rule 4: One vowel + one consonant ‚Üí double consonant + -est
big ‚Üí the biggest, hot ‚Üí the hottest, thin ‚Üí the thinnest
üí° Key Point:

Use superlative adjectives with "the" to compare three or more things. Short adjectives = add -est. Long adjectives = use "the most".

3

Irregular Comparatives and Superlatives

What are irregular comparatives and superlatives?

Some adjectives don't follow the regular rules. They have completely different forms for comparative and superlative. You need to memorize these!

üìã Common Irregular Forms

Irregular Comparatives and Superlatives:
good ‚Üí better ‚Üí best
bad ‚Üí worse ‚Üí worst
far ‚Üí farther/further ‚Üí farthest/furthest
little ‚Üí less ‚Üí least
much/many ‚Üí more ‚Üí most
old ‚Üí older/elder ‚Üí oldest/eldest
Examples:
This pizza is good, but that one is better. The best pizza is at Mario's.
The weather is bad today, but yesterday was worse. Last week was the worst.
I live far from school. She lives farther. He lives the farthest.
I have little money. You have less. He has the least.
I have many books. You have more. She has the most.

üìù Special Notes

Farther vs Further:
Farther = physical distance (The store is farther than the school.)
Further = abstract distance (We need to discuss this further.)
Older vs Elder:
Older = general use (She is older than me.)
Elder = family relationships (My elder brother lives in London.)
üí° Key Point:

Memorize irregular forms: good‚Üíbetter‚Üíbest, bad‚Üíworse‚Üíworst, far‚Üífarther‚Üífarthest, little‚Üíless‚Üíleast, much/many‚Üímore‚Üímost.

Adjective Order

What is adjective order?

When you use more than one adjective before a noun, they must follow a specific order. Native speakers follow this order naturally, but learners need to memorize it!

üìã The Order of Adjectives

1. Opinion (beautiful, ugly, nice, horrible)
2. Size (big, small, tall, short)
3. Age (old, new, young, ancient)
4. Shape (round, square, long, thin)
5. Color (red, blue, green, black)
6. Origin (American, French, Chinese, Italian)
7. Material (wooden, plastic, metal, cotton)
8. Purpose (sleeping bag, racing car, coffee cup)
Examples:
a beautiful big old red Italian car
a nice small round wooden table
a horrible tall thin man
a comfortable large new blue sofa
üí° Memory Tip:

Remember: O-S-A-S-C-O-M-P (Opinion, Size, Age, Shape, Color, Origin, Material, Purpose). You don't need to use all categories - just follow the order!

5

-ed vs -ing Adjectives

What's the difference between -ed and -ing adjectives?

Many adjectives can end in -ed or -ing. The difference is about who feels the emotion or quality.

üìù The Key Difference

-ed adjectives = describe how people feel
I am bored. (I feel bored.)
She is tired. (She feels tired.)
They are excited. (They feel excited.)
-ing adjectives = describe what causes the feeling
The movie is boring. (The movie causes boredom.)
The work is tiring. (The work causes tiredness.)
The news is exciting. (The news causes excitement.)

üìã Common -ed and -ing Adjectives

-ed: bored, tired, excited, interested, surprised, confused, worried, disappointed
-ing: boring, tiring, exciting, interesting, surprising, confusing, worrying, disappointing
More Examples:
I'm interested in science. Science is interesting.
She was surprised by the news. The news was surprising.
He is confused about the problem. The problem is confusing.
üí° Key Point:

-ed = how people feel (I am bored). -ing = what causes the feeling (The movie is boring).

Gradable vs Non-Gradable Adjectives

What are gradable and non-gradable adjectives?

Some adjectives can have different degrees (very, quite, extremely). Others are absolute - they're either true or false, with no degrees.

üìù Gradable Adjectives

Gradable adjectives can have different degrees. You can use very, quite, rather, extremely with them.

Examples:
very hot, quite tall, rather expensive, extremely beautiful
Common gradable adjectives: big, small, hot, cold, happy, sad, good, bad, interesting, boring

üìù Non-Gradable Adjectives

Non-gradable adjectives are absolute - they're either true or false. Use absolutely, completely, totally with them (NOT very).

Examples:
absolutely perfect, completely wrong, totally impossible
Common non-gradable adjectives: perfect, impossible, dead, alive, unique, empty, full, finished
üí° Key Point:

Gradable = use very/quite (very hot). Non-gradable = use absolutely/completely (absolutely perfect, NOT very perfect).

7

Too / Enough / Very / Quite

How to use Too, Enough, Very, and Quite

These words modify adjectives and have different meanings. Let's learn when to use each one!

üìù Too

Too = more than necessary, more than wanted (negative meaning).

Examples:
This coffee is too hot. (It's hotter than I want.)
The bag is too heavy. (It's heavier than I can carry.)
She is too young to drive. (She's younger than the required age.)

üìù Enough

Enough = sufficient, adequate. It comes after the adjective.

Examples:
This coffee is hot enough. (It's sufficiently hot.)
She is old enough to drive. (She's sufficiently old.)
I'm not tall enough to reach it. (I'm not sufficiently tall.)

üìù Very

Very = to a high degree, extremely (neutral meaning).

Examples:
This coffee is very hot. (It's extremely hot.)
She is very tall. (She's extremely tall.)
The test was very difficult. (It was extremely difficult.)

üìù Quite

Quite = fairly, rather, to a certain degree (moderate meaning).

Examples:
This coffee is quite hot. (It's fairly hot.)
She is quite tall. (She's rather tall.)
The test was quite difficult. (It was fairly difficult.)
üí° Key Points:
  • Too = more than wanted (too hot = hotter than I want)
  • Enough = sufficient (hot enough = sufficiently hot) - comes AFTER adjective
  • Very = extremely (very hot = extremely hot)
  • Quite = fairly/rather (quite hot = fairly hot)
