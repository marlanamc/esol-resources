Welcome! Let's Learn About Adverbs Together

We're going to learn about adverbs step by step. Don't worry - we'll practice each part before moving to the next. Take your time and work through each section!

What is an adverb? An adverb is a word that describes or modifies a verb, an adjective, or another adverb. It tells us how, when, where, how often, or to what degree something happens. Examples: quickly, always, very, here, carefully, often.

1

Adverbs of Manner

What are adverbs of manner?

Adverbs of manner describe how something happens or is done. They answer the question "How?" Most adverbs of manner are formed by adding -ly to adjectives.

üìù How to Form Adverbs of Manner

Most adjectives adjective + -ly
Adjectives ending in -y change -y to -ily
Regular Formation (add -ly):
quick ‚Üí quickly (She runs quickly.)
careful ‚Üí carefully (He drives carefully.)
slow ‚Üí slowly (Walk slowly.)
beautiful ‚Üí beautifully (She sings beautifully.)
Adjectives ending in -y (change to -ily):
easy ‚Üí easily (She solved it easily.)
happy ‚Üí happily (They lived happily.)
angry ‚Üí angrily (He spoke angrily.)
busy ‚Üí busily (She worked busily.)

üìù Irregular Adverbs of Manner

Some adverbs don't end in -ly:
well (not "goodly") - She speaks English well.
fast (same as adjective) - He runs fast.
hard (same as adjective) - She works hard.
late (same as adjective) - He arrived late.
üí° Key Point:

Adverbs of manner usually come after the verb or after the object. Most are formed by adding -ly to adjectives. Remember: good ‚Üí well (not "goodly").

2

Adverbs of Frequency

What are adverbs of frequency?

Adverbs of frequency tell us how often something happens. They answer the question "How often?" Common adverbs of frequency include: always, usually, often, sometimes, rarely, never.

üìù Common Adverbs of Frequency

100%: always
90%: usually, normally
70%: often, frequently
50%: sometimes
30%: occasionally
10%: rarely, seldom
0%: never
Examples:
I always brush my teeth in the morning.
She usually arrives on time.
We often go to the park.
They sometimes eat out.
He rarely watches TV.
I never smoke.

üìù Position of Adverbs of Frequency

With "be" verb: After the verb "be"
She is always late. (NOT: She always is late.)
They are usually happy.
With other verbs: Before the main verb
I always drink coffee. (NOT: I drink always coffee.)
She often goes shopping.
With auxiliary verbs: After the auxiliary verb
I have never been to Paris.
She can always help you.
üí° Key Point:

Adverbs of frequency usually come before the main verb, but after "be" verbs. Remember: "always" = 100%, "never" = 0%.

3

Adverbs of Degree

What are adverbs of degree?

Adverbs of degree tell us to what extent or how much something happens. They modify adjectives, verbs, or other adverbs. Common adverbs of degree include: very, quite, too, enough, extremely, rather, fairly, pretty, almost, completely.

üìù Common Adverbs of Degree

Very strong: extremely, absolutely, completely, totally
Strong: very, really, quite
Moderate: fairly, rather, pretty
Almost: almost, nearly
Too much: too
Sufficient: enough
Examples:
The test was very difficult.
She is extremely talented.
It's quite cold today.
The coffee is too hot.
I'm almost finished.
She's pretty good at math.

üìù Position of Adverbs of Degree

Before adjectives:
She is very beautiful.
It's quite expensive.
Before adverbs:
She speaks very quickly.
He drives quite carefully.
Before verbs:
I really like pizza.
She almost forgot.
Special: "Enough" comes AFTER:
She is old enough to drive.
I don't have money enough. (or: enough money)
üí° Key Point:

Adverbs of degree usually come before adjectives and adverbs, but "enough" comes after. "Very" = strong degree, "quite" = moderate degree, "too" = excessive.

4

Adverbs of Time

What are adverbs of time?

Adverbs of time tell us when something happens. They answer the question "When?" Common adverbs of time include: now, then, today, yesterday, tomorrow, soon, already, yet, still, before, after, early, late.

üìù Common Adverbs of Time

Present: now, today, currently
Past: yesterday, then, before, earlier
Future: tomorrow, soon, later, next
Duration: still, yet, already, just
Sequence: first, next, then, finally
Examples:
I'm studying now.
She arrived yesterday.
We'll leave soon.
Have you finished yet?
I've already eaten.
He's still working.

üìù Position of Adverbs of Time

Usually at the end:
I'll call you tomorrow.
She left yesterday.
Can be at the beginning:
Today, I'm going shopping.
Yesterday, we visited the museum.
"Still", "yet", "already" have special positions:
I'm still waiting. (after "be")
I still love you. (before main verb)
Have you finished yet? (at the end)
I've already done it. (after auxiliary)
üí° Key Point:

Adverbs of time usually come at the end of the sentence, but can come at the beginning for emphasis. "Still", "yet", and "already" have specific positions.

5

Adverbs of Place

What are adverbs of place?

Adverbs of place tell us where something happens. They answer the question "Where?" Common adverbs of place include: here, there, everywhere, somewhere, anywhere, nowhere, inside, outside, upstairs, downstairs, nearby, far, away.

üìù Common Adverbs of Place

Location: here, there
Direction: up, down, in, out, away, back
Indefinite: everywhere, somewhere, anywhere, nowhere
Position: inside, outside, upstairs, downstairs
Distance: nearby, far, away
Examples:
Come here.
Put it there.
I looked everywhere for my keys.
Let's go inside.
She lives nearby.
The store is far away.

üìù Position of Adverbs of Place

Usually at the end:
I'll meet you there.
Put the book here.
Can be at the beginning:
Here is your coffee.
There goes the bus.
After the object:
I put the keys inside.
She walked away.
üí° Key Point:

Adverbs of place usually come at the end of the sentence. "Here" and "there" can come at the beginning for emphasis.

6

Adverb Position

Where do adverbs go in a sentence?

Adverb position depends on the type of adverb and the type of verb in the sentence. Let's learn the general rules!

üìù General Rules for Adverb Position

1. With "be" verb: After "be"
She is always late.
They are here.
2. With other verbs: Before the main verb
I always drink coffee.
She often goes shopping.
3. With auxiliary verbs: After the auxiliary verb
I have never been to Paris.
She can always help you.
4. Adverbs of manner: Usually after the verb or object
She speaks quickly.
He drives the car carefully.
5. Adverbs of time/place: Usually at the end
I'll call you tomorrow.
Meet me here.
üí° Key Point:

Remember: After "be", before main verbs, after auxiliary verbs. Adverbs of manner go after verbs, adverbs of time/place go at the end.

7

Adverb Formation

How are adverbs formed?

Most adverbs are formed from adjectives by adding -ly. However, there are some spelling rules and exceptions to remember!

üìù Spelling Rules for -ly

Rule 1: Add -ly
quick ‚Üí quickly, careful ‚Üí carefully, slow ‚Üí slowly
Rule 2: Ends in -y ‚Üí change -y to -ily
easy ‚Üí easily, happy ‚Üí happily, angry ‚Üí angrily
Rule 3: Ends in -le ‚Üí change -le to -ly
simple ‚Üí simply, gentle ‚Üí gently, terrible ‚Üí terribly
Rule 4: Ends in -ic ‚Üí add -ally
basic ‚Üí basically, automatic ‚Üí automatically, dramatic ‚Üí dramatically

üìù Irregular Adverbs

Some adverbs don't follow the -ly rule:
good ‚Üí well (NOT "goodly")
fast ‚Üí fast (same as adjective)
hard ‚Üí hard (same as adjective)
late ‚Üí late (same as adjective)
early ‚Üí early (same as adjective)
üí° Key Point:

Most adverbs = adjective + -ly. Remember spelling changes: -y ‚Üí -ily, -le ‚Üí -ly, -ic ‚Üí -ally. Some adverbs are irregular (good ‚Üí well).
