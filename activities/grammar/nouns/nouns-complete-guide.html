What is a noun? A noun is a word that names a person, place, thing, or idea. Examples: teacher, city, book, happiness.

1

Countable vs Uncountable Nouns

What's the difference?

Some nouns you can count (one, two, three...) and some you cannot count. This is very important because it affects which articles and quantifiers you use!

üî¢ Countable Nouns

Countable nouns are things you can count. They have singular and plural forms.

Examples:
one book ‚Üí two books
one student ‚Üí three students
one apple ‚Üí five apples
one car ‚Üí many cars

üí° You can use numbers (1, 2, 3...) and "a/an" with countable nouns.

a/an + countable noun (singular)
countable noun + -s/-es (plural)

üíß Uncountable Nouns

Uncountable nouns are things you cannot count. They don't have plural forms and you cannot use "a/an" with them.

Common Categories:
Liquids: water, milk, coffee, juice
Food: rice, bread, cheese, sugar
Abstract: love, happiness, information, advice
Materials: wood, metal, glass, paper
Weather: rain, snow, sunshine
Subjects: English, math, history
Examples:
I need water. ‚úÖ (NOT "a water" or "waters")
She has information. ‚úÖ (NOT "an information" or "informations")
We bought rice. ‚úÖ (NOT "a rice" or "rices")

üí° Use "some", "a lot of", "much", or "a little" with uncountable nouns.

üí° Key Point:

Ask yourself: "Can I count this?" If yes ‚Üí countable. If no ‚Üí uncountable.

Remember:

  • Countable: a/an + singular, or plural form
  • Uncountable: no "a/an", no plural form, use "some" or "much"

2

Singular vs Plural

Understanding Singular and Plural

Singular means one. Plural means more than one. Most countable nouns form the plural by adding -s or -es.

üìù Regular Plural Rules

Rule 1: Add -s
book ‚Üí books
car ‚Üí cars
student ‚Üí students
teacher ‚Üí teachers
Rule 2: Add -es (for words ending in -s, -ss, -sh, -ch, -x, -z)
bus ‚Üí buses
class ‚Üí classes
dish ‚Üí dishes
box ‚Üí boxes
watch ‚Üí watches
Rule 3: Change -y to -ies (for words ending in consonant + y)
city ‚Üí cities
baby ‚Üí babies
country ‚Üí countries

üí° Exception: If the word ends in vowel + y, just add -s: day ‚Üí days, boy ‚Üí boys

Rule 4: Change -f/-fe to -ves
leaf ‚Üí leaves
knife ‚Üí knives
wife ‚Üí wives

üí° Exception: Some words just add -s: roof ‚Üí roofs, belief ‚Üí beliefs

üí° Key Point:

Most nouns follow these rules, but some have irregular plurals (we'll learn those next!).

3

Irregular Plurals

What are irregular plurals?

Some nouns don't follow the regular rules! They change in special ways. You need to memorize these.

üîÑ Common Irregular Plurals

Change the vowel:
man ‚Üí men
woman ‚Üí women
foot ‚Üí feet
tooth ‚Üí teeth
mouse ‚Üí mice
Add -en or change completely:
child ‚Üí children
ox ‚Üí oxen
Same form (singular = plural):
sheep ‚Üí sheep
deer ‚Üí deer
fish ‚Üí fish (or fishes)
aircraft ‚Üí aircraft
Words from Latin/Greek:
cactus ‚Üí cacti (or cactuses)
crisis ‚Üí crises
phenomenon ‚Üí phenomena
criterion ‚Üí criteria
üí° Key Point:

There's no easy rule for irregular plurals - you need to learn them! Practice makes perfect.

4

Compound Nouns

What are compound nouns?

Compound nouns are made from two or more words that work together as one noun. They can be written as one word, two words, or with a hyphen.

üîó Types of Compound Nouns

One word:
toothbrush (tooth + brush)
bedroom (bed + room)
notebook (note + book)
sunshine (sun + shine)
Two words:
post office (post + office)
ice cream (ice + cream)
bus stop (bus + stop)
credit card (credit + card)
Hyphenated:
mother-in-law (mother + in + law)
check-in (check + in)
well-being (well + being)
six-year-old (six + year + old)

üìù Plural Forms of Compound Nouns

Usually, make the main word plural (the most important word in the compound).

Examples:
toothbrush ‚Üí toothbrushes (main word: brush)
mother-in-law ‚Üí mothers-in-law (main word: mother)
passer-by ‚Üí passers-by (main word: passer)
bus stop ‚Üí bus stops (main word: stop)
üí° Key Point:

The main word is usually the last word in the compound. Make that word plural!

5

Collective Nouns

What are collective nouns?

Collective nouns name a group of people, animals, or things. They are singular in form but can refer to multiple things.

üë• Common Collective Nouns

Groups of People:
team (a team of players)
family (a family of four)
class (a class of students)
audience (an audience of people)
crew (a crew of workers)
Groups of Animals:
herd (a herd of cows)
flock (a flock of birds)
pack (a pack of wolves)
school (a school of fish)
pride (a pride of lions)
Groups of Things:
bunch (a bunch of grapes)
set (a set of tools)
collection (a collection of stamps)
pile (a pile of books)

üìù Singular or Plural?

Collective nouns can be tricky! In British English, they're often treated as plural. In American English, they're usually treated as singular.

American English (singular):
The team is playing well. ‚úÖ
My family lives in Boston. ‚úÖ
British English (can be plural):
The team are playing well. ‚úÖ
My family live in London. ‚úÖ
üí° Key Point:

In American English, treat collective nouns as singular (use "is", "has", etc.).

6

Proper Nouns vs Common Nouns

What's the difference?

Common nouns name general things. Proper nouns name specific people, places, or things and always start with a capital letter.

üìù Common Nouns

Common nouns are general names. They don't start with capital letters (unless at the beginning of a sentence).

Examples:
teacher (any teacher)
city (any city)
book (any book)
dog (any dog)
country (any country)

üèõÔ∏è Proper Nouns

Proper nouns are specific names. They always start with capital letters.

Examples:
Ms. Johnson (specific teacher)
New York (specific city)
Harry Potter (specific book)
Rover (specific dog's name)
Canada (specific country)

üìã Categories of Proper Nouns

People's Names:
John, Maria, Dr. Smith
Places:
Paris, Mount Everest, Pacific Ocean
Organizations:
United Nations, Microsoft, Harvard University
Days, Months, Holidays:
Monday, January, Christmas
Languages & Nationalities:
English, Spanish, American
üí° Key Point:

Proper nouns = specific names = capital letters. Common nouns = general names = lowercase (unless at sentence start).

Noun Formation

How are nouns formed?

Many nouns are formed by adding suffixes (endings) to verbs, adjectives, or other words. Learning these patterns helps you understand and create new nouns!

üî§ Common Noun Suffixes

-tion / -sion (action or process):
educate ‚Üí education
decide ‚Üí decision
inform ‚Üí information
discuss ‚Üí discussion
-ment (action or result):
develop ‚Üí development
govern ‚Üí government
improve ‚Üí improvement
move ‚Üí movement
-er / -or (person who does):
teach ‚Üí teacher
act ‚Üí actor
write ‚Üí writer
visit ‚Üí visitor
-ness (state or quality):
happy ‚Üí happiness
kind ‚Üí kindness
sad ‚Üí sadness
ill ‚Üí illness
-ity (state or quality):
active ‚Üí activity
real ‚Üí reality
possible ‚Üí possibility
curious ‚Üí curiosity
-ing (activity or result):
read ‚Üí reading
swim ‚Üí swimming
build ‚Üí building
paint ‚Üí painting
üí° Key Point:

Learning suffixes helps you understand new words! If you see "-tion" or "-ment", you know it's probably a noun.
