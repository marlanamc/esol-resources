Welcome! Let's Learn About Pronouns Together

We're going to learn about pronouns step by step. Don't worry - we'll practice each part before moving to the next. Take your time and work through each section!

What is a pronoun? A pronoun is a word that replaces a noun to avoid repetition. Instead of saying "John went to the store. John bought milk. John came home," we say "John went to the store. He bought milk. He came home." Examples: I, you, he, she, it, we, they, this, that, who, which.

1

Personal Pronouns

What are personal pronouns?

Personal pronouns replace people or things. They have different forms depending on whether they're the subject (doing the action) or object (receiving the action) of a sentence.

üë§ Subject Pronouns

Subject pronouns are used when the pronoun is the subject (who/what does the action) of the sentence.

Subject Pronouns:
I - first person singular
You - second person (singular/plural)
He - third person masculine singular
She - third person feminine singular
It - third person neuter singular
We - first person plural
They - third person plural
Examples:
I like pizza.
You are my friend.
He plays soccer.
She studies English.
It is raining.
We go to school.
They live here.

üéØ Object Pronouns

Object pronouns are used when the pronoun is the object (receives the action) of the sentence.

Object Pronouns:
Me - first person singular
You - second person (singular/plural)
Him - third person masculine singular
Her - third person feminine singular
It - third person neuter singular
Us - first person plural
Them - third person plural
Examples:
She called me.
I see you.
They helped him.
He loves her.
We found it.
She invited us.
I know them.
üí° Key Point:

Subject pronouns = who does the action (I, you, he, she, it, we, they). Object pronouns = who receives the action (me, you, him, her, it, us, them).

Possessive Pronouns

What are possessive pronouns?

Possessive pronouns show ownership or possession. They replace a noun + possessive adjective. For example, instead of saying "This is my book," you can say "This is mine."

üìã Possessive Pronouns

Possessive pronouns stand alone - they don't need a noun after them.

Possessive Pronouns:
Mine - my + noun
Yours - your + noun
His - his + noun
Hers - her + noun
Its - its + noun (rare)
Ours - our + noun
Theirs - their + noun
Examples:
This book is mine. (This book is my book.)
That car is yours. (That car is your car.)
The blue bag is hers. (The blue bag is her bag.)
These keys are theirs. (These keys are their keys.)
The house is ours. (The house is our house.)

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Confuse: Possessive Adjectives vs Possessive Pronouns

Possessive adjectives come BEFORE a noun. Possessive pronouns REPLACE the noun.

Comparison:
This is my book. (possessive adjective + noun)
This book is mine. (possessive pronoun - no noun needed)
That is your pen. (possessive adjective + noun)
That pen is yours. (possessive pronoun - no noun needed)
üí° Key Point:

Possessive pronouns = mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs. They stand alone - no noun after them!

3

Reflexive Pronouns

What are reflexive pronouns?

Reflexive pronouns refer back to the subject of the sentence. They're used when the subject and object are the same person or thing. They end in -self (singular) or -selves (plural).

ü™û Reflexive Pronouns

Reflexive Pronouns:
Myself - I
Yourself - you (singular)
Himself - he
Herself - she
Itself - it
Ourselves - we
Yourselves - you (plural)
Themselves - they
Examples:
I cut myself while cooking. (I cut I)
She taught herself English. (She taught she)
We enjoyed ourselves at the party. (We enjoyed we)
They hurt themselves playing soccer. (They hurt they)
You should take care of yourself. (You take care of you)

üìù When to Use Reflexive Pronouns

Use reflexive pronouns when:

  • The subject and object are the same person/thing
  • For emphasis (to emphasize who did something)
  • After certain verbs (enjoy, hurt, teach, cut, etc.)
For Emphasis:
I myself saw it happen. (I, and no one else)
The president himself came to the meeting. (The president, personally)
üí° Key Point:

Reflexive pronouns = myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves. Use them when the subject and object are the same!

Relative Pronouns & Relative Clauses

What are relative pronouns?

Relative pronouns connect a relative clause (a part of a sentence that gives more information) to the main sentence. They refer back to a noun mentioned earlier.

üîó Common Relative Pronouns

Relative Pronouns:
Who - for people (subject)
Whom - for people (object)
Which - for things/animals
That - for people/things/animals
Whose - shows possession
Where - for places
Examples:
The woman who lives next door is a teacher. (who = subject)
The book which I bought is interesting. (which = thing)
The car that he drives is red. (that = thing)
The student whose bag was stolen reported it. (whose = possession)
This is the place where I was born. (where = place)

üìù Who vs Whom

Who = subject (does the action). Whom = object (receives the action).

Examples:
The person who called me is my friend. (who = subject, does "called")
The person whom I called is my friend. (whom = object, receives "called")

üí° Tip: In modern English, "who" is often used instead of "whom" in informal speech, but "whom" is correct for formal writing.

üìù Which vs That

That = defining relative clause (essential information). Which = non-defining relative clause (extra information, needs commas).

Defining (That - no commas):
The book that I read was interesting. (essential - which book?)
The car that he bought is expensive. (essential - which car?)
Non-Defining (Which - with commas):
My car, which is red, is very fast. (extra info - we already know which car)
The book, which I finished yesterday, was great. (extra info)

üìù Defining vs Non-Defining Relative Clauses

Defining clauses give essential information (you can't remove them). Non-defining clauses give extra information (you can remove them, use commas).

Defining (no commas):
Students who study hard get good grades. (essential - which students?)
Non-Defining (with commas):
My brother, who lives in Paris, is a doctor. (extra info - we know who my brother is)
üí° Key Point:

Who/whom = people, Which = things, That = people/things, Whose = possession, Where = places. Use "that" for essential info, "which" with commas for extra info.

5

Demonstrative Pronouns

What are demonstrative pronouns?

Demonstrative pronouns point to or identify specific things. They show which thing you're talking about and whether it's near or far, singular or plural.

üìç Demonstrative Pronouns

Demonstrative Pronouns:
This - near, singular
That - far, singular
These - near, plural
Those - far, plural
Examples:
This is my book. (near me, one book)
That is your car. (far from me, one car)
These are my keys. (near me, multiple keys)
Those are your friends. (far from me, multiple friends)

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Confuse: Demonstrative Pronouns vs Demonstrative Adjectives

Demonstrative pronouns stand alone (no noun after them). Demonstrative adjectives come before a noun.

Comparison:
This is expensive. (pronoun - stands alone)
This book is expensive. (adjective - before noun)
Those are mine. (pronoun - stands alone)
Those shoes are mine. (adjective - before noun)
üí° Key Point:

This/That = singular (near/far), These/Those = plural (near/far). When they stand alone = pronouns. When before a noun = adjectives.

Indefinite Pronouns

What are indefinite pronouns?

Indefinite pronouns refer to people or things without being specific. They don't refer to a particular person or thing. Examples: some, any, no, every, each, all.

üî¢ Common Indefinite Pronouns

Some/Any/No/Every + body/one/thing/where:
Somebody/Someone - a person
Something - a thing
Somewhere - a place
Anybody/Anyone - any person
Anything - any thing
Anywhere - any place
Nobody/No one - no person
Nothing - no thing
Nowhere - no place
Everybody/Everyone - all people
Everything - all things
Everywhere - all places
Other Common Indefinite Pronouns:
All - everything/everyone
Each - every one separately
Both - two things together
Either - one of two
Neither - not one or the other
Many - a large number
Much - a large amount
Few - a small number
Little - a small amount
Examples:
Someone called you. (a person, not specific)
I need something to eat. (a thing, not specific)
Everyone is here. (all people)
I don't know anyone here. (any person)
Nothing happened. (no thing)

üìù Some vs Any

Some = positive sentences, offers, requests. Any = negative sentences, questions.

Examples:
I have some money. (positive)
I don't have any money. (negative)
Do you have any questions? (question)
Would you like some coffee? (offer)
üí° Key Point:

Indefinite pronouns = some, any, no, every, each, all + body/one/thing/where. They refer to non-specific people or things!

Review & Summary

Pronouns Review & Summary

Congratulations! You've learned about all the main types of pronouns. Let's review what you've covered:

üìö What You've Learned

  • 1. Personal Pronouns - Replace people/things (I, you, he, she, it, we, they, me, him, her, us, them)
    Subject: I like pizza. Object: She called me.
  • 2. Possessive Pronouns - Show ownership (mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs)
    This book is mine. That car is yours.
  • 3. Reflexive Pronouns - Refer back to subject (myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves)
    I cut myself. She taught herself English.
  • 4. Relative Pronouns - Connect clauses (who, whom, which, that, whose, where)
    The woman who lives next door is a teacher. The book which I bought is interesting.
  • 5. Demonstrative Pronouns - Point to things (this, that, these, those)
    This is my book. Those are your keys.
  • 6. Indefinite Pronouns - Refer to non-specific people/things (some, any, no, every, each, all + body/one/thing/where)
    Someone called you. I need something to eat.
    • üí° Key Reminders:
    • Subject pronouns = who does the action (I, you, he, she, it, we, they)
    • Object pronouns = who receives the action (me, you, him, her, it, us, them)
    • Who = subject, Whom = object (for people)
    • That = essential info, Which = extra info (with commas)
    • This/That = singular, These/Those = plural
    • Some = positive, Any = negative/questions
