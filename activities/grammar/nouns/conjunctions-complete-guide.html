Welcome! Let's Learn About Conjunctions Together

We're going to learn about conjunctions step by step. Don't worry - we'll practice each part before moving to the next. Take your time and work through each section!

What is a conjunction? A conjunction is a word that connects words, phrases, or clauses in a sentence. Conjunctions help us join ideas together and make our sentences more interesting and complex. Examples: and, but, or, so, because, although, if.

1

Coordinating Conjunctions

What are coordinating conjunctions?

Coordinating conjunctions connect words, phrases, or independent clauses (complete sentences) of equal importance. They join similar grammatical elements. The most common coordinating conjunctions are: and, but, or, so, for, yet, nor.

üìù The Seven Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS)

Remember: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So

AND - Adds information (addition):
‚Ä¢ I like tea and coffee. (connects two nouns)
‚Ä¢ She is smart and kind. (connects two adjectives)
‚Ä¢ I went to the store, and I bought some milk. (connects two sentences)
BUT - Shows contrast (opposition):
‚Ä¢ I wanted to go, but I was too tired.
‚Ä¢ He's rich but not happy.
‚Ä¢ She tried hard, but she didn't succeed.
OR - Shows choice (alternative):
‚Ä¢ Would you like tea or coffee?
‚Ä¢ You can come with us or stay here.
‚Ä¢ Finish your homework, or you can't go out.
SO - Shows result (consequence):
‚Ä¢ It was raining, so I took an umbrella.
‚Ä¢ I was tired, so I went to bed early.
‚Ä¢ She studied hard, so she passed the exam.
FOR - Shows reason (cause):
‚Ä¢ I stayed home, for I was feeling sick.
‚Ä¢ She couldn't come, for she had to work.
Note: "For" is more formal and less common than "because"
YET - Shows contrast (similar to "but"):
‚Ä¢ He's young, yet he's very experienced.
‚Ä¢ It's expensive, yet it's worth it.
NOR - Shows negative alternative:
‚Ä¢ I don't like tea, nor do I like coffee.
‚Ä¢ She can't swim, nor can she ride a bike.
Note: "Nor" requires inversion (do I, can she)
üí° Key Point:

When joining two independent clauses (complete sentences) with a coordinating conjunction, use a comma before the conjunction. Example: "I like pizza, but my friend prefers pasta."

2

Subordinating Conjunctions

What are subordinating conjunctions?

Subordinating conjunctions connect a dependent clause (incomplete sentence) to an independent clause (complete sentence). They show the relationship between the two clauses - such as time, cause, condition, contrast, or purpose.

üìù Common Subordinating Conjunctions by Function

Time (when something happens):
‚Ä¢ when - I'll call you when I arrive.
‚Ä¢ while - She called while I was cooking.
‚Ä¢ before - Finish your homework before you go out.
‚Ä¢ after - After I finish work, I'll go home.
‚Ä¢ since - I've lived here since 2020.
‚Ä¢ until - Wait until I come back.
‚Ä¢ as soon as - Call me as soon as you arrive.
Cause/Reason (why something happens):
‚Ä¢ because - I stayed home because I was sick.
‚Ä¢ since - Since it's raining, let's stay inside.
‚Ä¢ as - As you're here, let's start the meeting.
Condition (if something happens):
‚Ä¢ if - If it rains, we'll stay home.
‚Ä¢ unless - I won't go unless you come too.
‚Ä¢ as long as - You can come as long as you're quiet.
‚Ä¢ provided that - I'll help provided that you try.
Contrast (showing difference):
‚Ä¢ although - Although it was cold, we went out.
‚Ä¢ though - Though she's young, she's very smart.
‚Ä¢ even though - Even though I was tired, I kept working.
‚Ä¢ whereas - I like tea, whereas my friend prefers coffee.
‚Ä¢ while - While I like action movies, she prefers comedies.
Purpose (why something is done):
‚Ä¢ so that - I studied hard so that I could pass.
‚Ä¢ in order that - She left early in order that she wouldn't be late.
Other common subordinating conjunctions:
‚Ä¢ than - She's taller than I am.
‚Ä¢ as - Do as I say.
‚Ä¢ where - This is where I was born.
üí° Key Point:

When a subordinating conjunction starts a sentence, use a comma after the dependent clause. Example: "Although it was raining, we went out." But: "We went out although it was raining." (no comma)

3

Correlative Conjunctions

What are correlative conjunctions?

Correlative conjunctions are pairs of conjunctions that work together to connect words, phrases, or clauses. They always come in pairs and must be used together. Common correlative conjunctions include: both...and, either...or, neither...nor, not only...but also, whether...or.

üìù Common Correlative Conjunctions

BOTH...AND - Shows two things together:
‚Ä¢ I like both tea and coffee.
‚Ä¢ She is both smart and kind.
‚Ä¢ Both my parents and my sister came to the party.
EITHER...OR - Shows choice between two options:
‚Ä¢ You can have either tea or coffee.
‚Ä¢ Either you come with us or you stay here.
‚Ä¢ I'll see you either today or tomorrow.
NEITHER...NOR - Shows negative choice (both are not true):
‚Ä¢ I like neither tea nor coffee.
‚Ä¢ Neither my brother nor my sister came.
‚Ä¢ She can neither swim nor ride a bike.
NOT ONLY...BUT ALSO - Emphasizes two things:
‚Ä¢ She is not only smart but also kind.
‚Ä¢ He speaks not only English but also French.
‚Ä¢ Not only did she finish early, but she also helped others.
WHETHER...OR - Shows two alternatives:
‚Ä¢ I don't know whether to go or stay.
‚Ä¢ Whether you like it or not, you have to do it.
‚Ä¢ Tell me whether you're coming or not.
üí° Key Point:

Correlative conjunctions must be used in pairs. Make sure both parts are present and that the structure after each part is parallel (the same grammatical form).

Common Conjunction Errors

Common mistakes with conjunctions

Many English learners make mistakes with conjunctions. Here are the most common errors and how to avoid them!

üìù Common Errors to Avoid

Error 1: Using "because" and "so" together
‚ùå I was tired, because so I went to bed.
‚úÖ I was tired, so I went to bed.
‚úÖ I went to bed because I was tired.
Remember: Use "because" OR "so", not both!
Error 2: Using "although" and "but" together
‚ùå Although it was cold, but we went out.
‚úÖ Although it was cold, we went out.
‚úÖ It was cold, but we went out.
Remember: Use "although" OR "but", not both!
Error 3: Missing comma with coordinating conjunctions
‚ùå I wanted to go but I was too busy.
‚úÖ I wanted to go, but I was too busy.
Remember: Use a comma before coordinating conjunctions when joining two independent clauses!
Error 4: Confusing "because" and "so"
‚ùå I was late so I missed the bus.
‚úÖ I was late because I missed the bus. (reason)
‚úÖ I missed the bus, so I was late. (result)
Remember: "Because" = reason, "So" = result!
Error 5: Incomplete correlative conjunctions
‚ùå I like both tea and coffee.
‚úÖ I like both tea and coffee.
‚ùå You can have either tea or coffee.
‚úÖ You can have either tea or coffee.
Remember: Correlative conjunctions must come in pairs!
üí° Key Point:

Never use "because" with "so" or "although" with "but" - they mean the same thing! Use commas before coordinating conjunctions when joining two sentences. Always use both parts of correlative conjunctions!
