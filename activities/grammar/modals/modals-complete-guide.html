Welcome! Let's Learn Modal Verbs Together

We're going to learn modal verbs step by step. Modal verbs are special verbs that help us express ability, possibility, obligation, advice, and more. Don't worry - we'll practice each part before moving to the next!

What are modal verbs? They are helping verbs that add meaning to other verbs. Examples: can, could, may, might, must, should, will, would.

What Are Modal Verbs? Understanding the Basics

Why are modal verbs important?

Modal verbs help us express ability, possibility, obligation, advice, permission, and certainty. They add important meaning to our sentences!

üìå Key Characteristics of Modal Verbs:

  • 1. They don't change form - Modal verbs stay the same for all subjects (I, you, he, she, it, we, they)
    I can swim. / He can swim. / They can swim.
    I must go. / She must go. / We must go.

    üí° Notice: No -s for he/she/it! Modal verbs don't change.

  • 2. They are followed by base verbs - After a modal, use the base form of the verb (no "to")
    I can speak English. ‚úÖ
    I can to speak English. ‚ùå
    She should study more. ‚úÖ
    She should studies more. ‚ùå
  • 3. They form questions and negatives differently - No "do/does/did" needed!
    Can you help me? (NOT: Do you can help me?)
    I cannot swim. / I can't swim. (NOT: I don't can swim.)
    Should we go? (NOT: Do we should go?)
    • üí° Key Point:

    Modal verbs are helping verbs that add meaning. They always come before another verb and never change form!

    1

    Expressing Ability: Can / Could / Be able to

    When do we use these?

    We use can, could, and be able to to talk about ability - what someone is capable of doing.

    Can (Present Ability)

    Use: For present ability or general ability

    I can speak Spanish.
    She can drive a car.
    They can swim.

    Could (Past Ability)

    Use: For past ability or polite requests

    When I was young, I could run fast.
    She could play piano when she was 10.
    Could you help me? (polite request)

    Be able to (All Tenses)

    Use: When you need other tenses (future, perfect, etc.)

    I will be able to come tomorrow.
    She has been able to finish the project.
    I was able to solve the problem.
    Subject + can/could + base verb
    Subject + be able to + base verb
    üí° Remember:
    • Can = present ability
    • Could = past ability OR polite request
    • Be able to = use for future, perfect tenses, or when "can" doesn't work
    • Negative: cannot / can't or could not / couldn't

    2

    Expressing Possibility: May / Might / Could

    When do we use these?

    We use may, might, and could to talk about possibility - things that might happen or could be true.

    May (Possible, Formal)

    Use: For possibility (more formal) or permission

    It may rain tomorrow.
    She may be late.
    May I come in? (permission)

    Might (Less Certain)

    Use: For possibility (less certain than "may")

    It might snow tonight.
    He might not come to the party.
    They might be at home.

    Could (Possible, Less Certain)

    Use: For possibility (similar to "might")

    It could be true.
    She could arrive late.
    They could win the game.
    Subject + may/might/could + base verb
    üí° Remember:
    • May = possible (more formal, 50% certain)
    • Might = less certain (30-40% certain)
    • Could = possible (similar to might, 30-50% certain)
    • All three express possibility, but might is usually less certain
    Practice: May, Might, or Could?

    Choose the best modal to express possibility.

    3

    Expressing Obligation: Must / Have to / Should / Ought to

    When do we use these?

    We use these modals to express obligation, necessity, and advice - things you need to do or should do.

    Must (Strong Obligation, Personal)

    Use: Strong obligation from the speaker's opinion

    You must see this movie!
    I must finish this today.
    Students must wear uniforms.

    Have to (External Obligation)

    Use: Obligation from rules, laws, or external sources

    I have to work tomorrow.
    She has to pay taxes.
    We have to follow the rules.

    Should (Advice, Recommendation)

    Use: For advice or recommendations (not as strong as "must")

    You should study more.
    He should see a doctor.
    They should arrive early.

    Ought to (Formal Advice)

    Use: Similar to "should" but more formal

    You ought to apologize.
    She ought to be more careful.
    We ought to help them.
    Subject + must/should/ought to + base verb
    Subject + have/has to + base verb
    üí° Remember:
    • Must = strong obligation (personal opinion)
    • Have to = external obligation (rules, laws)
    • Should = advice/recommendation (less strong)
    • Ought to = formal advice (similar to "should")
    • Negative: mustn't (prohibition) vs don't have to (no obligation)
    Practice: Must, Have to, Should, or Ought to?

    Choose the best modal to express obligation or advice.

    4

    Need to / Don't need to

    When do we use these?

    Need to expresses necessity. Don't need to means there's no necessity (but you can do it if you want).

    Need to (Necessity):
    I need to buy groceries.
    She needs to study for the exam.
    We need to leave soon.
    Don't need to (No Necessity):
    You don't need to come early. (You can come early if you want, but it's not necessary)
    They don't need to bring food. (Food is optional)
    He doesn't need to work tomorrow. (It's optional)
    üí° Important Difference:
    • Don't need to = no necessity (but you can do it)
    • Mustn't = prohibition (you cannot do it)
    • Example: "You don't need to bring a gift" (optional) vs "You mustn't bring alcohol" (forbidden)

    Complete the sentences with the correct form.

    5

    Had better (Strong Advice/Warning)

    When do we use "had better"?

    Had better is used for strong advice or warnings. It suggests there will be negative consequences if you don't follow the advice.

    Examples:
    You had better study for the test. (or you'll fail)
    We had better leave now. (or we'll be late)
    She had better apologize. (or there will be problems)
    I had better go. (or I'll miss my bus)
    Subject + had better + base verb
    üí° Remember:
    • Had better = strong advice with implied warning
    • It's always "had better" (not "have better" or "has better")
    • Negative: had better not (You had better not be late!)
    • Similar to "should" but stronger - suggests consequences

    6

    Modal Passives

    What are modal passives?

    Modal passives combine modal verbs with passive voice. We use them when we want to focus on the action rather than who does it.

    Subject + modal verb + be + past participle
    Examples:
    The work must be finished by Friday.
    This problem should be solved immediately.
    The report can be submitted online.
    The door might be locked.
    The exam has to be taken in person.
    üí° Remember:
    • Structure: Modal + be + past participle
    • Use when the action is more important than who does it
    • Common in formal writing, rules, and instructions

    7

    Expressing Degrees of Certainty

    How certain are you?

    Modal verbs can express different levels of certainty - from very certain to very uncertain.

    Certainty Scale:

    100% Certain (Must):
    She must be tired. (I'm very certain)
    He must know the answer. (I'm sure)
    90% Certain (Should/Ought to):
    They should be here soon. (very likely)
    It ought to work. (very likely)
    50% Certain (May/Could):
    It may rain. (possible)
    She could be at home. (possible)
    30% Certain (Might):
    He might come. (less certain)
    They might be late. (less certain)
    üí° Remember:
    • Must = very certain (90-100%)
    • Should/Ought to = very likely (80-90%)
    • May/Could = possible (40-60%)
    • Might = less certain (20-40%)
