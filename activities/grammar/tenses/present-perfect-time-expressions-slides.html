Why Are These Words Important?

PAST
→
NOW
→
FUTURE

Time expressions are VERY common with Present Perfect! They help you understand when and how to use the tense correctly.

FOR + period (for 5 years, for 2 months)
SINCE + starting point (since 2020, since Monday)
ALREADY = before now (affirmative sentences)
YET = until now (negative sentences & questions)
JUST = a short time ago (affirmative sentences)

FOR vs SINCE

FOR = a period of time

Duration (how long)

Use FOR with a duration:

for 5 years
for 2 months
for a week
for a long time
for 10 minutes

I have lived here for 5 years.

✓ Still living here now
VS

SINCE = a starting point

Starting Point (when)

Use SINCE with a point in time:

since 2020
since January
since last week
since I was a child
since Monday

I have lived here since 2020.

✓ Still living here now
Memory Trick:

FOR = "how long" (duration) | SINCE = "when it started" (point in time)

Practice: FOR or SINCE?

Complete each sentence with the correct time expression:

  1. I have lived here _____ (2020).
  2. She has studied English _____ (2 years).
  3. We have been friends _____ (childhood).
  4. They have worked here _____ (6 months).
  5. He has had this job _____ (January).
Answers:
1. since | 2. for | 3. since | 4. for | 5. since

ALREADY

"before now"

Position

Mid-position

have/has + already + past participle

Used in

Affirmative sentences

Positive statements

Examples:

I have already eaten lunch.
She has already finished her work.
We have already visited Paris.
Tip: "Already" means the action happened before you expected it to happen.

YET

"until now"

Position

End position

At the end of the sentence

Used in

Negative sentences & questions

Not in affirmative statements

Examples:

I haven't finished my homework yet.
She hasn't arrived yet.
Have you seen that movie yet?
Warning: Never use "yet" in affirmative sentences!

JUST

"a short time ago"

Position

Mid-position

have/has + just + past participle

Used in

Affirmative sentences

Positive statements only

Examples:

I have just finished my homework.
She has just arrived home.
They have just bought a new car.
Tip: "Just" emphasizes that the action happened very recently.

EVER

"at any time in your life"

Position

Mid-position

have/has + ever + past participle

Used in

Questions & negative sentences

Not in affirmative statements

Examples:

Have you ever been to Paris?
I have never been to Paris.
Has she ever eaten sushi?
Tip: "Ever" in questions asks about life experiences.

NEVER

"not at any time in your life"

Position

Mid-position

have/has + never + past participle

Used in

Negative sentences

Only in negative statements

Examples:

I have never been to Paris.
She has never eaten sushi.
They have never visited Europe.
Remember: "Never" = "not ever" - the opposite of "ever".

ALREADY vs YET

ALREADY

Affirmative sentences

I have already finished my work.

✓ Before expected time
VS

YET

Negative & Questions

I haven't finished my work yet.

Have you finished your work yet?

✓ Until now
Key difference: Already = completed action | Yet = action not completed

EVER vs NEVER

EVER

Questions & Negatives

Have you ever been to Paris?

✓ "At any time in life"
VS

NEVER

Negative statements

I have never been to Paris.

✓ "Not at any time"
Memory trick: EVER = questions, NEVER = negative statements

Practice: ALREADY

Complete with "already":

  1. I _____ (finish) my homework.
  2. She _____ (eat) lunch.
  3. We _____ (see) that movie.
  4. He _____ (arrive) at work.
  5. They _____ (buy) their tickets.

1. have already finished | 2. has already eaten | 3. have already seen | 4. has already arrived | 5. have already bought

Mixed Practice

Choose the correct time expression:

  1. I haven't eaten breakfast _____.
  2. Have you _____ been to Japan?
  3. She has _____ finished her work.
  4. They _____ arrived at the airport.
  5. We have _____ seen that movie.

Choices: already, yet, just, ever, never

Answers:
1. yet | 2. ever | 3. already | 4. have just | 5. never

Summary

ALREADY = affirmative sentences, "before now"
YET = negative sentences & questions, "until now"
JUST = affirmative sentences, "a short time ago"
EVER = questions & negatives, "at any time"
NEVER = negative sentences, "not at any time"
