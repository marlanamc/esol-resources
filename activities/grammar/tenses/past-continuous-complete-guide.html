What is Past Continuous? It's used to talk about actions that were in progress at a specific time in the past, interrupted actions, and parallel actions happening at the same time.

When Do We Use Past Continuous? Understanding the Meaning

Why is meaning important?

Knowing the structure (how to form sentences) is important, but understanding the meaning (when and why to use Past Continuous) helps you use it correctly in real life!

üìå Past Continuous is used for:

  • 1. Actions in Progress at a Specific Time in the Past - Actions that were happening at a particular moment in the past
    At 3 PM yesterday, I was reading a book.
    She was studying when I called her.
    They were playing soccer at 5 PM yesterday.

    üí° These describe actions that were in progress at a specific past time.

  • 2. Interrupted Actions - An action that was in progress when another action happened
    I was cooking dinner when the phone rang.
    She was working when her friend arrived.
    They were sleeping when the alarm went off.

    üí° The Past Continuous action (was cooking) is interrupted by a Past Simple action (rang).

  • 3. Parallel Actions - Two or more actions happening at the same time in the past
    While I was studying, my brother was watching TV.
    She was cooking while he was setting the table.
    They were talking while we were working.

    üí° These show two actions happening simultaneously in the past.

  • 4. Background Information in Stories - Setting the scene or providing context
    The sun was shining, and birds were singing.
    It was raining when we left the house.
    People were walking in the park.

    üí° Past Continuous describes the background or atmosphere in past narratives.

  • 5. Repeated Actions in the Past - Actions that happened repeatedly over a period in the past
    Last summer, I was working at a restaurant.
    During that time, she was studying for her exams.
    They were living in London last year.

    üí° These describe ongoing situations or activities during a past period.

    • üí° Key Point:

    Past Continuous describes actions that were in progress at a specific time in the past. It's often used with Past Simple to show interrupted or parallel actions.

    Compare:

    • "I worked at a school" (Past Simple - completed action)
    • "I was working at a school when I met her" (Past Continuous - action in progress)

    Time Expressions - Your Clues!

    Why are time expressions important?

    Time expressions are like clues that help you know which tense to use! When you see these words, they tell you if something was happening at a specific time in the past or was interrupted by another action.

    üìÖ Time Expressions for Past Continuous

    These time expressions help you identify Past Continuous. When you see them, think: "This was happening at a specific time in the past!"

    Common Time Expressions:
    At 3 PM yesterday - specific past time
    Yesterday - past day
    Last week/month/year - past period
    While - during (parallel actions)
    When - at the time (interrupted actions)
    At that time - specific past moment
    During - throughout a period
    As - at the same time
    All morning/afternoon - duration in past
    Examples:
    I was studying at 3 PM yesterday.
    She was working when I called.
    We were learning English last year.
    They were playing while we were studying.
    He was living in Boston during that time.
    üí° Key Point:

    When you see time expressions like "at 3 PM yesterday", "yesterday", "last week", "while", "when", "during" ‚Üí think Past Continuous!

    Compare:

    • "I worked yesterday" (Past Simple - completed action)
    • "I was working at 3 PM yesterday" (Past Continuous - action in progress)

    State Verbs vs Action Verbs - Why We Can't Say "I Was Liking"

    What's the difference?

    Some verbs describe states (how things are) and some describe actions (what we do). This is important because state verbs don't use continuous forms - only action verbs can use Past Continuous!

    üî¥ State Verbs (No Continuous Forms)

    State verbs describe states, feelings, thoughts, or senses. These verbs are NOT used in continuous forms (not "was liking", "was knowing", "was wanting").

    Common State Verbs:
    Like, love, hate, prefer (feelings)
    Know, understand, believe (thoughts)
    Want, need, own (possession/desire)
    See, hear, smell, taste (senses)
    Be, seem, appear (states)
    Have (when meaning "own")
    Examples (Correct):
    I liked chocolate. ‚úÖ (NOT "I was liking")
    She knew the answer. ‚úÖ (NOT "She was knowing")
    They wanted to go. ‚úÖ (NOT "They were wanting")
    I understood English. ‚úÖ (NOT "I was understanding")

    üí° These verbs describe states, so we use Past Simple, not Past Continuous.

    üü¢ Action Verbs (Can Use Continuous Forms)

    Action verbs describe actions or activities. These verbs CAN use continuous forms when the action was in progress in the past.

    Common Action Verbs:
    Run, walk, jump (movement)
    Eat, drink, cook (activities)
    Study, work, read (activities)
    Play, watch, listen (activities)
    Write, speak, talk (communication)
    Examples:
    I ate breakfast yesterday. (Past Simple - completed action)
    I was eating breakfast at 8 AM. (Past Continuous - action in progress) ‚úÖ
    She worked at a hospital. (Past Simple - completed action)
    She was working when I called. (Past Continuous - action in progress) ‚úÖ
    üí° Key Point:

    State verbs describe how things ARE (states, feelings, thoughts). Action verbs describe what we DO (activities, movements).

    Remember:

    • State verbs ‚Üí Use Past Simple only (I liked, I knew, I wanted) - NO continuous forms!
    • Action verbs ‚Üí Can use Past Simple (completed actions) OR Past Continuous (actions in progress)
    • Never say: "I was liking", "I was knowing", "I was wanting" ‚ùå
    • Can say: "I was eating", "I was studying", "I was working" ‚úÖ (action verbs)
    Practice: State Verb or Action Verb?

    Read each sentence and decide if the verb is a state verb or action verb. Remember: state verbs don't use continuous forms!

    Past Continuous - Positive (Affirmative) Sentences

    What are positive sentences?

    Positive sentences tell us what someone was doing at a specific time in the past. They're not negative (saying "no") and they're not questions.

    I/He/She/It + was + verb + -ing
    You/We/They + were + verb + -ing
    ‚ö†Ô∏è Important: We use was with I/he/she/it, and were with you/we/they!
    Look at these examples:
    I was studying at 3 PM yesterday. (I + was + verb-ing)
    She was working when I called. (She + was + verb-ing)
    They were playing soccer. (They + were + verb-ing)
    He was reading a book. (He + was + verb-ing)
    We were learning English last year. (We + were + verb-ing)
    Maria was writing an email. (Maria = she, was + verb-ing)
    üí° Remember:
    • I, he, she, it ‚Üí use was + verb-ing
    • You, we, they ‚Üí use were + verb-ing
    • For verbs ending in -e, drop the -e and add -ing (write ‚Üí writing, make ‚Üí making)
    • For verbs ending in consonant-vowel-consonant, double the last letter (run ‚Üí running, sit ‚Üí sitting)

    Past Continuous - Negative Sentences

    What are negative sentences?

    Negative sentences tell us what someone wasn't doing at a specific time in the past. We add not after was/were.

    I/He/She/It + was not/wasn't + verb + -ing
    You/We/They + were not/weren't + verb + -ing
    ‚ö†Ô∏è Important: We can use contractions: wasn't (was not) and weren't (were not)!
    Look at these examples:
    I wasn't studying at that time. (I + wasn't + verb-ing)
    She wasn't working yesterday. (She + wasn't + verb-ing)
    They weren't playing soccer. (They + weren't + verb-ing)
    He was not reading a book. (He + was not + verb-ing)
    We weren't learning Spanish. (We + weren't + verb-ing)
    Maria wasn't writing an email. (Maria + wasn't + verb-ing)
    üí° Remember:
    • I, he, she, it ‚Üí use wasn't or was not + verb-ing
    • You, we, they ‚Üí use weren't or were not + verb-ing

    Past Continuous - Questions

    How do we make questions?

    To make questions, we start with Was or Were, then add the subject, then the verb + -ing.

    Was + I/he/she/it + verb + -ing ?
    Were + you/we/they + verb + -ing ?
    ‚ö†Ô∏è Important: After Was/Were, we use the verb + -ing form!
    Look at these examples:
    Were you studying at that time? (Were + you + verb-ing)
    Was she working yesterday? (Was + she + verb-ing)
    Were they playing soccer? (Were + they + verb-ing)
    Was he reading a book? (Was + he + verb-ing)
    Were we learning English? (Were + we + verb-ing)
    Was Maria writing an email? (Was + Maria + verb-ing)
    üí° Remember:
    • I, he, she, it ‚Üí start with Was
    • You, we, they ‚Üí start with Were
    • After Was/Were, use verb + -ing
    • Don't forget the question mark ?
