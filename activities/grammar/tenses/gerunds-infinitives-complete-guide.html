Welcome! Let's Learn Gerunds and Infinitives Together

We're going to learn when to use gerunds (-ing forms) and infinitives (to + verb) step by step. This is an important grammar topic that will help you speak and write more naturally in English!

What are gerunds and infinitives?

  • Gerund: A verb form ending in -ing that acts as a noun (e.g., "I enjoy swimming")
  • Infinitive: The base form of a verb with to (e.g., "I want to swim")
1

What Are Gerunds?

Understanding Gerunds

A gerund is a verb form that ends in -ing and functions as a noun. This means it can be the subject, object, or complement of a sentence.

Verb + -ing = Gerund
Examples:
Swimming is good exercise. (subject)
I enjoy reading. (object)
My favorite hobby is cooking. (complement)
She's interested in learning English. (after preposition)

Gerunds look like Present Continuous verbs (swimming, reading), but they function as nouns, not verbs. They answer "what?" instead of "what is happening?"

2

What Are Infinitives?

Understanding Infinitives

An infinitive is the base form of a verb preceded by to. It can function as a noun, adjective, or adverb.

to + base verb = Infinitive
Examples:
To learn English is my goal. (subject)
I want to travel around the world. (object)
This is a good place to study. (adjective - describes "place")
She came to help us. (adverb - tells why she came)
ðŸ’¡ Key Point:

Infinitives often express purpose or intention. They answer "why?" or "for what purpose?"

Common Uses of Gerunds

    She put off studying until tomorrow.

    It's worth visiting that museum.

    When to Use Infinitives

    Common Uses of Infinitives

    Verbs That Can Take Both (With Different Meanings)

    Important: Some Verbs Can Take Both!

    Some verbs can be followed by either a gerund or an infinitive, but the meaning changes! This is very important to understand.

  • 1. Remember
    I remember meeting him. (past memory - I have a memory of meeting him)
    Remember to call me! (future action - don't forget to call)
  • 2. Forget
    I'll never forget visiting Paris. (past memory - I remember visiting)
    Don't forget to buy milk! (future action - don't forget to do this)
  • 3. Stop
    I stopped smoking. (stopped the activity of smoking)
    I stopped to smoke. (stopped in order to smoke - purpose)
  • 4. Try
    Try pressing this button. (experiment with this action)
    I tried to open the door. (attempted to do this)
  • 5. Regret
    I regret telling him. (I'm sorry I told him - past action)
    I regret to inform you... (I'm sorry to have to tell you - formal, future action)
    • ðŸ’¡ Key Point:

    When the meaning changes, gerunds usually refer to past actions or experiences, while infinitives usually refer to future actions or purposes.

    Practice: Choose the Correct Form

    Choose the correct form based on the meaning. Think about whether it's a past memory/experience (gerund) or future action/purpose (infinitive).
