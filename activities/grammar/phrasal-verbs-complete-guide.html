What are Phrasal Verbs? Phrasal verbs are combinations of a verb + preposition or verb + adverb that create a new meaning. They're very common in English, especially in informal conversation. For example: "look up" (search for), "give up" (quit), "turn on" (activate).

Transitive vs Intransitive Phrasal Verbs

What's the difference?

Understanding transitive and intransitive phrasal verbs helps you know whether you need an object or not. This is very important for using phrasal verbs correctly!

üü¢ Transitive Phrasal Verbs

Need an object - They have a direct object

I turned on the light.
She looked up the word.
They put away their books.

üí° You can ask: "Turned on what?" ‚Üí The light

üîµ Intransitive Phrasal Verbs

No object needed - They don't have a direct object

The plane took off.
He showed up late.
The meeting broke up.

üí° You cannot ask "Took off what?" - no object!

üí° Key Point:

Transitive phrasal verbs need an object (what or who receives the action). Intransitive phrasal verbs don't need an object - the sentence is complete without one.

Test: If you can ask "verb + what/whom?" and get an answer, it's transitive!

Separable vs Inseparable Phrasal Verbs

Why is this important?

For transitive phrasal verbs, you need to know if you can separate the verb and the particle (preposition/adverb). This affects word order!

üü¢ Separable Phrasal Verbs

Object can go between verb and particle OR after the particle

I turned the light on. ‚úÖ
I turned on the light. ‚úÖ
She put her books away. ‚úÖ
She put away her books. ‚úÖ

üí° With pronouns, you must separate: "Turn it on" ‚úÖ (NOT "Turn on it" ‚ùå)

üîµ Inseparable Phrasal Verbs

Object must go after the particle - cannot separate

I looked after the children. ‚úÖ
I looked the children after. ‚ùå
She ran into her friend. ‚úÖ
She ran her friend into. ‚ùå

üí° The verb and particle always stay together

üí° Key Point:

For separable phrasal verbs: If the object is a pronoun (it, them, him, her), you must put it between the verb and particle.

Examples:

  • "Turn it on" ‚úÖ (NOT "Turn on it" ‚ùå)
  • "Put them away" ‚úÖ (NOT "Put away them" ‚ùå)

Common Phrasal Verbs

Essential Phrasal Verbs to Know

Here are some of the most common phrasal verbs in English. Learning these will help you understand and use English more naturally!

look up
to search for information
I need to look up that word in the dictionary.
give up
to quit, to stop trying
Don't give up! Keep trying!
turn on
to activate, to start
Please turn on the lights.
turn off
to deactivate, to stop
Don't forget to turn off the TV.
put away
to store, to tidy
Please put away your toys.
pick up
to lift, to collect
Can you pick up some milk?
get up
to rise from bed
I get up at 7 AM every day.
wake up
to stop sleeping
I usually wake up early.
look after
to take care of
Can you look after my cat?
find out
to discover
I need to find out the answer.
show up
to arrive, to appear
He didn't show up to the meeting.
break down
to stop working, to fail
My car broke down yesterday.

Phrasal Verbs with Multiple Meanings

One Phrasal Verb, Different Meanings

Many phrasal verbs have more than one meaning. The context helps you understand which meaning is being used!

  • break up
    1. The meeting broke up at 5 PM. (ended)
    2. They broke up last month. (ended their relationship)
    3. Can you break up the chocolate? (divide into pieces)
  • pick up
    1. Can you pick up the pen? (lift)
    2. I'll pick up some groceries. (buy)
    3. Can you pick me up at 8? (collect)
    4. I picked up Spanish quickly. (learned)
  • turn on
    1. Please turn on the TV. (activate)
    2. That music really turns me on. (excites, attracts - informal)
  • get over
    1. I need to get over this wall. (climb over)
    2. She can't get over her ex-boyfriend. (recover from)
    3. I can't get over how beautiful it is! (be surprised by)
  • run into
    1. I ran into my friend at the store. (met by chance)
    2. The car ran into a tree. (collided with)
    • üí° Key Point:

    Always pay attention to the context to understand which meaning of a phrasal verb is being used. The same phrasal verb can mean very different things!

    Verb + Preposition Combinations

    Common Verb + Preposition Patterns

    Some verbs are always followed by specific prepositions. These combinations are fixed - you can't change the preposition!

    Verb + Preposition + Object

    Common Patterns:

  • believe in - to have faith in
    I believe in you!
  • depend on - to rely on
    I depend on my car to get to work.
  • listen to - to pay attention to sound
    I like to listen to music.
  • wait for - to stay until something happens
    I'm waiting for the bus.
  • think about - to consider
    I need to think about it.
  • apologize for - to say sorry for
    I apologize for being late.
  • succeed in - to achieve success in
    She succeeded in passing the exam.
    • üí° Key Point:

    These verb + preposition combinations are fixed. You must use the correct preposition - you can't change it!

    Remember: "listen to" (NOT "listen at"), "depend on" (NOT "depend of")
