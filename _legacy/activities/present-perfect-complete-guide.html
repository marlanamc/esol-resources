Welcome! Let's Learn Present Perfect Together

We're going to learn Present Perfect step by step. This guide has numbered sections so you can easily navigate and say "go to section 3" or "let's work on section 5."

What is Present Perfect? It connects the past to the present. We use it for past actions that have a connection to now.

1

Section 1: When Do We Use Present Perfect?

Understanding the meaning first

Present Perfect connects the past to the present. It's used when something happened in the past, but we care about it NOW.

Present Perfect is used for:

  • 1. Life Experiences - Things you have done in your life (up to now)
    I have visited Paris.
    She has tried sushi before.

    The exact time doesn't matter - it's about the experience you have now.

  • 2. Recent Actions with Results Now - Something just happened and affects the present
    I have lost my keys. (I don't have them NOW)
    She has finished her homework. (It's done NOW)

    The action happened in the past, but the result is important NOW.

  • 3. Unfinished Time Periods - Actions in a time period that isn't finished yet
    I have studied English for 3 years. (still studying)
    We have lived here since 2020. (still living here)

    The time period continues to NOW.

    • Key Idea:

    Present Perfect connects PAST actions to the PRESENT moment. Think: "How does this past action matter NOW?"

    Section 2: Understanding Past Participles

    What is a Past Participle?

    Before we learn the formula, we need to understand past participles. These are special verb forms we use with "have" and "has".

    Two Types:

    Regular Verbs (add -ed):
    work → worked
    play → played
    visit → visited
    finish → finished
    Irregular Verbs (must memorize):
    go → gone
    see → seen
    eat → eaten
    be → been
    have → had
    do → done
    make → made
    take → taken
    Remember:
    • Regular verbs: add -ed (work → worked)
    • Irregular verbs: you need to memorize them
    • The past participle looks the same as past simple for regular verbs
    • But for irregular verbs, it's often different (went vs. gone)

    Section 3: Present Perfect vs. Past Simple

    When to use each?

    This is one of the most important things to understand!

    Present Perfect = Connection to NOW

    I have lost my keys. (I still don't have them)
    She has lived here for 5 years. (She still lives here)

    We care about the present situation, not the exact time in the past.

    Past Simple = Finished time in the past

    I lost my keys yesterday. (specific past time)
    She lived there in 2015. (finished time period)

    We know exactly when it happened, and it's finished.

    Quick Test:

    Can you say WHEN it happened (yesterday, last week, in 2020)?

    • YES → Use Past Simple
    • NO (or it doesn't matter) → Use Present Perfect

    Section 4: Positive Form (Statements)

    How to form Present Perfect statements

    Now let's learn the formula for making positive sentences in Present Perfect.

    Subject have/has past participle

    When to use "have" vs "has":

    Use "have" with: I, you, we, they
    I have worked here for 5 years.
    You have done a great job.
    We have seen that movie.
    They have visited Paris.
    Use "has" with: he, she, it
    He has finished his homework.
    She has lived here since 2020.
    It has been a long day.
    Remember:

    The formula is always: Subject + have/has + past participle

    Section 5: Negative Form

    How to make negative sentences

    To make Present Perfect negative, add "not" after have/has.

    Subject have/has not past participle
    Short forms: haven't and hasn't
    Examples with contractions:
    I haven't finished my homework yet.
    She hasn't visited Japan.
    They haven't seen that movie.
    He hasn't been to Paris.
    We haven't eaten lunch yet.
    Remember:
    • haven't = have not
    • hasn't = has not
    • We usually use contractions in speaking and informal writing

    Section 6: Question Form

    How to make questions

    To make Present Perfect questions, put have/has before the subject.

    Have/Has subject past participle ?
    Yes/No Questions:
    Have you finished your homework?
    Has she visited Japan?
    Have they seen that movie?
    Has he been to Paris?
    Short Answers:
    Yes, I have. / No, I haven't.
    Yes, she has. / No, she hasn't.
    Yes, they have. / No, they haven't.
    Remember:

    Questions follow the pattern: Have/Has + subject + past participle + ?

    Section 7: For & Since

    Using "for" and "since" with Present Perfect

    These words tell us HOW LONG something has continued.

    FOR = a period of time

    Use "for" when you say how many days, weeks, months, years, etc.

    I have lived here for 3 years.
    She has worked there for 6 months.
    They have known each other for a long time.

    SINCE = a starting point

    Use "since" when you say when something started (a specific time).

    I have lived here since 2020.
    She has worked there since January.
    They have known each other since high school.
    Quick Test:
    • Can you count it? (3 years, 2 months) → Use FOR
    • Is it a specific time point? (2020, Monday, breakfast) → Use SINCE

    Section 8: Already, Yet & Just

    Time words for recent actions

    These words tell us about the timing of recent actions.

    ALREADY = sooner than expected

    Use in positive sentences. Goes before the past participle OR at the end.

    I have already finished my homework.
    She has finished her homework already.

    YET = up to now (expecting it to happen)

    Use in negative sentences and questions. Goes at the end.

    I haven't finished my homework yet.
    Have you finished your homework yet?

    JUST = a very short time ago

    Use in positive sentences. Goes before the past participle.

    I have just finished my homework.
    She has just arrived.
    Quick Reference:
    • Already = positive sentences (done sooner than expected)
    • Yet = negative sentences & questions (still waiting)
    • Just = positive sentences (very recently)

    9

    Section 9: Ever & Never

    Talking about life experiences

    Use "ever" and "never" when talking about experiences in your whole life.

    EVER = at any time in your life

    Use in questions. Goes before the past participle.

    Have you ever visited Paris?
    Has she ever tried sushi?
    Have they ever been to Japan?

    NEVER = not at any time in your life

    Use in positive sentences (never is already negative). Goes before the past participle.

    I have never visited Paris.
    She has never tried sushi.
    They have never been to Japan.
    Important:
    • Ever is usually used in questions
    • Never is used in positive sentence structure (because "never" is already negative)
    • Don't say: "I haven't never..." (double negative) ✗
    • Say: "I have never..." ✓

    Section 11: Summary - What You've Learned

    Key Points to Remember

  • Form: Subject + have/has + past participle

    Example: I have finished, She has seen

  • Meaning: Connects the past to the present

    Use when the past action matters NOW

  • For vs. Since:

    FOR = period of time (3 years)
    SINCE = starting point (2020)

  • Already / Yet / Just:

    ALREADY = sooner than expected (positive)
    YET = still waiting (negative & questions)
    JUST = very recently (positive)

  • Ever / Never:

    EVER = in questions about life experiences
    NEVER = in positive sentences (already negative)

  • Negative: have/has + not + past participle

    Example: I haven't finished, She hasn't seen

  • Questions: Have/Has + subject + past participle?

    Example: Have you finished? Has she seen?

    • Final Tip:

    Always ask yourself: "Does this past action connect to NOW?" If yes, use Present Perfect!
