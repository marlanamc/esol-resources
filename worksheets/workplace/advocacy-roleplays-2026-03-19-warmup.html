Part B: Phrases

8 min

Read the examples. Practice using modals for obligation and permission in advocacy situations. Try to use vocabulary from Part A.

1) I must / have to… (obligation)
  • I must report unsafe working conditions. (my obligation)
  • I have to follow company safety rules. (requirement)
Your turn:
2) Can I / May I…? (asking permission)
  • Can I take my scheduled break? (informal request)
  • May I speak with HR about my rights? (formal/polite request)
Your turn:
3) You should / You are allowed to… (advice/permission)
  • You should document any violations of your rights. (advice)
  • You are allowed to refuse unsafe work. (permission/right)
Your turn:
4) You must not / You cannot… (prohibition)
  • You must not work without proper safety equipment. (prohibited)
  • You cannot be forced to work overtime without notice. (not allowed)
Your turn:
5) I need to… / I am entitled to…
  • I need to advocate for my rights. (necessity)
  • I am entitled to fair treatment at work. (right)
Your turn:

Level up: Use "be permitted to" for formal permission: "Employees are permitted to take breaks according to policy."

Reading

Self-advocacy means speaking up for your rights at work. Understanding modals helps you advocate effectively. Use "must" and "have to" to talk about obligations: "I must report safety violations." Use "can" and "may" to ask for permission: "May I take my break?" Use "should" to give advice: "You should document problems."

When advocating, know what you must do, what you are allowed to do, and what you should do. Good advocacy means understanding your rights (what you can do), your obligations (what you must do), and your responsibilities (what you should do).

1) How do modals help you advocate for your rights?
2) What is the difference between "I must report this" and "I should report this"?
3) When advocating, why is it important to know what you are allowed to do versus what you must do?

Writing Homework

6–8 sentences
Prompt: Imagine an employee's supervisor refuses to let them take a legally required 15-minute break after working 4 hours. The employee needs to talk to HR about this. Write a workplace advocacy conversation using modals. Include what the employee must do, what they are allowed to do, and what they should do. Show how modals express obligations and permissions.

Optional challenge: Use "be required to" (formal obligation) and "be entitled to" (right) in your conversation.
