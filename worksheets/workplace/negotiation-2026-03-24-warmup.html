Part B: Phrases

8 min

Read the examples. Practice using infinitives (to + verb) and gerunds (verb + -ing).

1) I'd like to… (infinitive)
  • I'd like to change my schedule.
  • I'd like to discuss my hours with you.
Your turn:
2) I need to… (infinitive)
  • I need to pick up my child at 3pm.
  • I need to improve my computer skills.
Your turn:
3) I enjoy… (gerund)
  • I enjoy working with customers.
  • I enjoy starting early in the morning.
Your turn:
4) Would you consider…? (gerund)
  • Would you consider changing my shift?
  • Would you consider giving me more hours?
Your turn:
5) I decided to… (infinitive)
  • I decided to ask for a schedule change.
  • I decided to speak with my manager about my concerns.

Level up: Use both infinitives and gerunds in one sentence: "I enjoy working here, but I need to change my hours."

Part C: Partner Practice

5 min
Round 1: Role-play: Employee asks for a schedule change using infinitives (I need to…, I'd like to…).
Round 2: Switch. Manager responds using gerunds: "Would you consider working weekends?"

Tips: Be polite, explain your reasons, and offer a compromise.

Reading

Negotiation means discussing to reach an agreement. When you negotiate at work, use polite language. Infinitives express goals: "I want to work mornings." Gerunds express preferences: "I enjoy working with people."

Some verbs take infinitives (decide to, plan to, need to). Others take gerunds (enjoy, avoid, consider). Practice both!

1) What is negotiation?
2) Give an example of a verb that takes an infinitive.
3) Give an example of a verb that takes a gerund.

Writing Homework

6–8 sentences
Prompt: Write a workplace negotiation conversation. Use both infinitives and gerunds to explain what you need, enjoy, and would like to change.
  • Try using infinitives (I need to, I'd like to, I plan to)
  • Try using gerunds (I enjoy, Would you consider)
  • You might include a polite request or a possible compromise
  • Try using vocabulary from Part A

Optional challenge: Use "avoid" (I can't avoid working late) and "suggest" (I suggest meeting weekly).
