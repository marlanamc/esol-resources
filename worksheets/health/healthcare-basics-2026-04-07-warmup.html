Reading

When you visit a doctor, you use many different grammar structures. Gerunds after verbs: "I avoid eating late" or "I'm considering seeing a doctor." Conditionals give or ask for advice: "If you have a fever, you should rest." Modals (should, must, can) show rules or suggestions: "You must take this with food." Phrasal verbs like come down with, get over, or give up are common: "I came down with a cold." Reported speech passes on what someone said: "The doctor said I should rest."

This week we're reviewing these structures (we'll do the big tense review next week). The more clearly you can describe your symptoms and what others told you, the better care you will get.

1) Why is it important to describe symptoms clearly to a doctor?
2) Name two different grammar structures you can use when talking about health (gerunds, conditionals, modals, phrasal verbs, or reported speech), and give an example of each.
3) What does "The doctor said I should rest" show? (Reported speech—who said it? What was the advice?)

Writing Homework

6–8 sentences
Prompt: Describe a health situation (real or imagined): symptoms, a visit to the doctor, or taking care of yourself. Example: "I avoid eating late when I'm sick. If I have a fever, I should rest. The doctor said I must take the medicine twice a day."

Optional challenge: Add reported speech (e.g., "My friend said she...") or another phrasal verb (take care of, look after, cut down on).
