Reading

When you get medicine from the pharmacy, understanding modals helps you follow instructions safely. Modals show different levels: "should" gives advice (recommended), "must" shows requirement or caution (required/prohibited), and "can/may" shows permission or possibility (allowed/might happen).

For example, "You should take this with food" is advice. "You must not drink alcohol" is a warning/caution. "You may feel drowsy" shows possibility. "You can take it with or without food" shows permission. Understanding these differences helps you follow instructions correctly and stay safe. Always ask the pharmacist if you don't understand something.

1) What is the difference between "You should take this with food" and "You must not drink alcohol"?
2) When would you use "may" versus "can" in pharmacy instructions?
3) Why is it important to understand the difference between advice, caution, and consent when taking medicine?

Writing Homework

6–8 sentences
Prompt: Imagine you work at a pharmacy. A customer asks about ibuprofen for headaches. They want to know how to take it safely. Write pharmacy instructions using modals. What should patients do? (advice) What must they do or not do? (caution) What can they do? (consent/permission) Use modals to explain the instructions clearly.

Optional challenge: Include warnings using "must not" and possibilities using "may" for side effects.
