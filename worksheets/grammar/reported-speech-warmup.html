Reading

Reported speech (also called indirect speech) is used to tell someone what another person said without quoting their exact words. Instead of saying, 'The doctor said, "You need to rest,"' you say, 'The doctor said that I need to rest.' The word 'that' is optional but helps clarify reported speech.

When reporting speech, pronouns and sometimes verb tenses change. Direct: 'I am busy.' Reported: 'She said that she was busy.' Direct: 'Do you have insurance?' Reported: 'They asked if I had insurance.' Use 'said' for statements, 'told' when there's an indirect object (told me, told him), and 'asked' for questions.

Reported speech is very useful in professional settings. After a doctor's appointment, you might tell your family what the doctor said. After a job interview, you might tell a friend what the interviewer asked. This skill helps you relay important information clearly and accurately.

Writing Homework

6–8 sentences
Prompt: Use reported speech to describe a recent conversation or message. This could be from a doctor visit, work, school, or a phone call. What did the person say? What did they ask? What did they tell you to do?
  • Try using "said that" for statements
  • Consider using "told me that" when someone told you specifically
  • Try using "asked if" for yes/no questions
  • Could use "wanted to know" for information requests

Optional challenge: Report a complete conversation with back-and-forth exchanges (what they said, what you said, what they responded).
